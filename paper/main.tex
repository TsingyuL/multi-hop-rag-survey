% ---------- Abstract -------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
Multi-hop retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) must recover an evidence chain whose later units become identifiable only after intermediate states are resolved. A system, however, observes only a noisy retrieval pool, a budgeted context, an evidence ordering, and a generated answer. Existing surveys organize this field primarily by architecture; such taxonomies catalogue systems effectively but do not isolate why they fail. We recast multi-hop RAG as latent evidence-chain inference under missingness, noise, budget, exposure, fusion, and faithfulness constraints. Along a statistical axis, we decompose grounded success into five conditional stage factors: chain observability, selection preservation, exposure probability, fusion reliability, and causal faithfulness. This yields an exact chain-rule factorization and a conditional bottleneck bound, while separating grounded success from shortcut answers and parametric leakage. Conditional evidence utility is treated as a mechanism-level score for improving selection rather than as an additional probability factor. Along a complementary architectural axis, we review retrieval, graph, decomposition, reader, agentic, long-context, multimodal, and mechanistic approaches through 2026. We then reinterpret benchmarks as diagnostic instruments, align empirical claims with conditional subsets and budgets, and propose an auditable reporting standard. A versioned companion artifact separates the broad imported literature library from the reviewed method and benchmark seeds, making the scope and maturity of the released taxonomy explicit.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% =========
Modern information retrieval systems are increasingly asked to answer complex questions whose evidence is distributed across many sources. A multi-hop retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) system is therefore rarely asked to retrieve a single fact; it is asked to recover an unobserved support structure. For a question $q$, the correct answer depends on a latent evidence chain $\echain^\star=(e_1^\star,\ldots,e_m^\star)$ whose elements must be discovered, ordered, and composed, while the system observes only a noisy retrieval pool $L_N$, a budgeted context $E_K$ $\subseteq$ $L_N$, an ordering $\pi(E_K)$, and the answer distribution induced by a reader. This mismatch between the latent object required for correctness and the observable objects available to the system is the central statistical difficulty of multi-hop RAG. Figure~\ref{fig:latent_chain_view} summarizes the latent target, observable pipeline, and five stage diagnostics.

\paperfigure{1.0}{fig1.pdf}
{Latent evidence-chain inference view of multi-hop RAG. The target object is an unobserved support chain $\echain^\star$, while the system observes only a retrieved pool $L_N$, a budgeted context $E_K$, an ordering $\pi(E_K)$, and a generated answer $\hat a$. The five estimands diagnose where the chain becomes absent, dropped, poorly exposed, incorrectly fused, or used only post hoc rather than causally.}
{fig:latent_chain_view}



Consider a question whose answer requires resolving an unstated bridge entity before the answer-bearing evidence can even be located. A single-shot retriever sees only the surface form of the original query and may retrieve highly relevant first-hop documents while missing the bridge document needed for the second hop. Increasing retrieval depth can make the full chain observable but inflates distractor mass; selecting a small context reduces noise but may discard a low-marginal-relevance bridge; reordering the selected evidence can make the chain easier for the reader to use but cannot recover evidence that was never selected. Even when the chain is present and visible, a fluent reader may answer from parametric memory or produce a plausible-looking rationale that is not causally responsible for the answer. These are not isolated engineering issues; they are successive statistical bottlenecks in the estimation and use of a latent support chain.

Existing surveys usually organize this field by architectural community: graph-based reading comprehension, iterative retrieval, question decomposition, fusion-in-decoder readers, chain-of-thought prompting, and agentic search~\cite{gao2023ragsurvey,fan2024ragllmsurvey,
mavi2022mhqasurvey,mohammadi2024mhmrcsurvey,plaat2024multistepsurvey,singh2025agenticragsurvey,liang2025reasoningragsurvey}. This organization helps locate papers but offers little explanation of why systems fail. Two methods with different surface architectures may target the same statistical bottleneck; conversely, two methods in the same family may fail for different statistical reasons. An iterative retriever, a graph expander, and an agentic search policy all attempt to increase the probability that the latent chain is observable. A reranker, a bridge selector, and a compression model all estimate some version of conditional evidence utility. Verification and counterfactual attribution, although discussed under distinct labels, both target the causal faithfulness of the final answer.

This survey therefore asks a different organizing question: \emph{which quantity of the latent evidence-chain inference problem does a method estimate?} The goal is not to discard architectural taxonomies but to place them under a more diagnostic statistical layer. We use five estimands as primary coordinates: chain observability, selection preservation under budget, exposure probability, fusion reliability, and causal faithfulness. Conditional evidence utility is treated as a mechanism-level selector score rather than as a sixth estimand. These quantities convert common multi-hop failure modes into measurable events and make design tradeoffs explicit. To preserve continuity with the existing literature, Section~\ref{sec:architecture_view} provides a parallel architectural review that catalogues methods by their surface design and maps each family to the estimands it primarily affects.


\subsection{A Running Example through the Latent-Chain Lens}
\label{subsec:running_example}
To make the distinction concrete, we adopt a canonical bridge-type
question from the HotpotQA benchmark~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa}, whose
gold supporting facts are annotated and publicly verifiable:
\begin{quote}
\emph{``What government position was held by the woman who portrayed
Corliss Archer in the film Kiss and Tell?''}
\end{quote}
A surface retriever conditioned on the literal query is drawn toward
passages about the 1945 film \emph{Kiss and Tell}, but the latent
support chain contains at least two dependent units:
$e_1^\star$, the bridge-entity unit establishing that Shirley~Temple
portrayed Corliss~Archer in \emph{Kiss and Tell}; and
$e_2^\star$, the answer-bearing unit stating that Shirley~Temple
subsequently served as United~States Chief of Protocol. The second
unit is not directly queryable from the surface form of the question;
it becomes reachable only after the bridge entity has been resolved.
We use this canonical two-hop example throughout the survey. When
discussing longer dependency chains, we use MuSiQue~\cite{trivedi2022musique}
as a benchmark family rather than introducing an additional untracked
running item. In
Section~\ref{sec:problem_setup} the example grounds the formalism
$(\echain^\star, L_N, E_K, \pi(E_K), \hat a)$; in
Section~\ref{sec:stat_decomposition} it illustrates each of the five
estimands; in Sections~\ref{sec:estimand_methods}
and~\ref{sec:architecture_view} it shows how different method
families intervene at different points; in
Section~\ref{sec:benchmarks} it anchors the discussion of which
benchmarks expose which failure events; and in
Section~\ref{sec:causal_faithfulness} it supports counterfactual
faithfulness illustrations.

In the notation used in this survey, the complete support chain
$\echain^\star$ is not a document list returned by a system; it is
the unobserved object that would make the answer identifiable. The
retrieved pool $L_N$ is an imperfect observation of that object, the
selected context $E_K$ is a budgeted estimator, and the reader-facing
ordering $\pi(E_K)$ is a presentation of the estimator. This
separation matters because different failures produce the same final
symptom. If the answer is wrong, it may be because the Shirley~Temple
biography passage was absent from $L_N$, because it was dropped when
$E_K$ was selected under a small context budget, because it was
placed far from the \emph{Kiss and Tell} cast passage in $\pi(E_K)$,
because the reader failed to compose the two passages, or because
the model answered from memorized knowledge without using the chain.
Final answer accuracy alone cannot distinguish these cases.

This example also clarifies why the statistical view is not merely a renaming of a pipeline. The latent chain induces a dependency structure, whereas the observed documents are sampled, selected, and ordered under system-specific policies. A retrieval module, a graph module, and an agentic search policy can therefore be compared by the same question: how much do they increase the probability that $\echain^\star$ becomes observable and usable under a fixed budget?


\subsection{Position Relative to Existing Surveys}
\label{subsec:survey_position}

Prior surveys have covered general RAG \RAG~\cite{gao2023ragsurvey,fan2024ragllmsurvey,huang2024ragsurvey,zhao2024ragaigcsurvey,li2024ragdecoupledsurvey}, multi-hop QA~\cite{mavi2022mhqasurvey,mohammadi2024mhmrcsurvey}, complex KBQA~\cite{lan2021complexkbqasurvey,gu2022knowledgesurvey}, knowledge-graph reasoning with \LLMs~\cite{niu2025kgreasoningsurvey,pan2024llmkgsurvey},
chain-of-thought and step-by-step reasoning~\cite{plaat2024multistepsurvey,chu2024cotsurvey,
qiao2023reasoningsurvey}, hallucination~\cite{huang2023hallucinationsurvey,zhang2023hallucinationsurvey}, faithfulness and attribution~\cite{li2023attributionsurvey,gao2023ragsurvey}, and agentic \RAG~\cite{singh2025agenticragsurvey,liang2025reasoningragsurvey,xi2023agentsurvey}. A particularly close recent work is the four-axis framework for retrieval--reasoning processes in multi-hop \QA~\cite{ji2026retrievalreasoning}, which organizes systems by execution plan, index structure, next-step control, and stopping criterion. These surveys are complementary to ours but answer a different question. Their primary axes are typically retrieval mechanism, system architecture, reasoning style, data source, control flow, or application domain. The unit of analysis is procedural: \emph{what does the system do at each step?} We add an estimand-based axis whose unit of analysis is statistical: \emph{which latent quantity does the step attempt to estimate or improve?}

Two procedures with different executions can target the same estimand, and one procedure can target several at once. An agentic loop may simultaneously increase pool observability, estimate conditional evidence utility, and improve faithfulness through critique; conversely, two iterative retrieval systems may both use repeated search but differ sharply in whether they optimize bridge observability or process faithfulness. The two views thus support different reviewer questions: a procedural view asks whether a method has a clear plan, index, trigger, and stopping rule; a latent-chain view asks whether the method improves $P(\echain^\star\subseteq L_N)$, preserves the chain under budget, exposes it early, composes it reliably, or uses it causally. We argue that a mature multi-hop RAG evaluation should report both, and this survey is structured so that the two views appear side by side.
The individual ingredients of our framing are not new in isolation: retrieved documents have been treated as latent variables in end-to-end RAG~\cite{lewis2020rag,guu2020realm}; multi-hop QA has long used evidence and reasoning chains~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa,trivedi2022musique,
ho2020wikimultihopqa}; and recent attribution work connects faithfulness to causal evidence use~\cite{xu2025veritas,wu2024cofca,liu2023evaluating}. Our contribution is to connect these ingredients into a single statistical organization of multi-hop RAG and to use that organization to support a comprehensive literature review whose method descriptions remain familiar to readers used to architectural categories.

Table~\ref{tab:survey_crosswalk} makes this positioning concrete by
tabulating which analytical axes each recent survey adopts. Existing
surveys already cover retrieval mechanisms, architectural taxonomies,
reasoning styles, and process control comprehensively. Two axes,
however, are largely absent from prior work and are the primary
contribution of the present survey: a statistical
\emph{estimand axis} that assigns each method to the latent quantity
it estimates or improves, and a matching \emph{estimand-aligned reporting
standard} that ties reported metrics to those quantities. A causal
reading of faithfulness (as counterfactual chain use rather than as
citation overlap) also appears only sporadically in prior surveys.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{subsec:contributions}
This survey makes four contributions.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em]
\item \textbf{A latent evidence-chain formalism.} We recast multi-hop RAG as inference over an unobserved support chain $\echain^\star$, with retrieved pools, selected contexts, evidence orderings, and generated answers treated as noisy observations or budgeted estimators of different aspects of that chain. We give both a latent-variable generative formulation and a Markov decision process view that subsumes recent agentic and adaptive systems.

\item \textbf{An estimand-based decomposition with diagnostic bounds.} We introduce five statistical estimands—chain observability, selection preservation under budget, exposure probability, fusion reliability, and causal faithfulness—and derive an exact chain-rule decomposition of grounded success over their conditional factors. A bottleneck upper bound shows that grounded success cannot exceed the smallest conditional stage-survival factor. Conditional evidence utility remains a mechanism-level scoring quantity for improving selection preservation, not a probability factor in the decomposition. We also state identifiability conditions linking observable diagnostics to latent events.

\item \textbf{A dual-axis review with auditable artifact boundaries.} We review methods, benchmarks, and analyses along two complementary axes: an estimand axis that identifies what statistical quantity each method targets, and an architectural axis (Section~\ref{sec:architecture_view}) that catalogues methods by graph reasoning, retrieval pipelines, question decomposition, fusion readers, LLM reasoning, agentic search, and hybrid LLM--KG approaches. To avoid inflating coverage, Section~\ref{sec:survey_methodology} distinguishes the manuscript bibliography, the broad imported working library, and the smaller reviewed catalog seed; only the last supports released taxonomy-level judgments. Coverage extends through 2024--2026 advances including process-supervised retrieval, RL-based search agents, long-context reading, multimodal multi-hop reasoning, mechanistic interpretation, latent reasoning, and retrieval-side safety.

\item \textbf{Statistical reinterpretation of benchmarks and open problems.} We reinterpret benchmarks as diagnostic instruments that make different latent events observable, propose a reporting standard that aligns metrics with estimands, and recast common open problems as tradeoffs among missingness, noise, budget, exposure, fusion error, and causal grounding.
\end{itemize}

\section{Survey Methodology and Literature Coverage}
\label{sec:survey_methodology}
% ==========================================

A survey that proposes a new statistical organization must make its own
selection procedure explicit. We screened papers along two dimensions:
(i) whether the work directly addresses multi-step evidence dependency,
and (ii) which latent-chain bottleneck it primarily targets. The first
criterion prevents the survey from becoming a generic \RAG or generic
\LLM-reasoning survey; the second prevents the taxonomy from collapsing
into a list of architectural names.

\subsection{Search and Inclusion Protocol}
\label{subsec:search_protocol}

The seed pool was assembled from ACL Anthology, OpenReview, arXiv,
publisher and venue proceedings pages, and official project repositories,
with DBLP and Semantic Scholar used as secondary discovery aids. We used
keyword families covering multi-hop question answering, complex question
answering, retrieval-augmented generation, iterative retrieval,
evidence retrieval, graph reasoning, \KGQA, chain-of-thought,
agentic search, process supervision, hallucination repair, citation
faithfulness, counterfactual evaluation, long-context reading, and
mechanistic interpretation of multi-hop reasoning. A paper was included
when it satisfied at least one of the following conditions: it proposes
a multi-hop method; it introduces a benchmark that exposes multi-step
evidence dependency; it analyzes faithfulness or causal support in a
retrieval-grounded setting; it is a foundational method that later
multi-hop \RAG systems reuse as a component; or it provides
interpretability evidence about how multi-hop reasoning is implemented
inside neural models.

We deliberately exclude papers that are only tangentially related. A
single-hop retriever is discussed when it forms the upstream pool for a
multi-hop system, but not as a full survey object. A general agent paper
is discussed only if tool use is used to improve evidence acquisition,
reasoning, or verification. A \KG embedding method is discussed only
when it functions as part of retrieval, graph expansion, or evidence
fusion for multi-hop answering. This scope keeps the survey centered on
latent evidence-chain inference rather than on every method that can be
placed near \RAG.

Candidate papers were assigned a primary architectural family (one of:
retrieval, graph/\KG, decomposition, fusion reader, \LLM reasoning,
agentic, hybrid, benchmark, or analysis) and a primary estimand (one of:
observability, selection, exposure, fusion, faithfulness, or joint).
Conditional evidence utility is recorded as a mechanism-level selection
signal rather than as a separate estimand. When a paper targeted multiple
estimands, the earliest bottleneck deliberately targeted by its central
mechanism was marked as primary and material downstream effects were
recorded as secondary. Records are promoted from the imported library to
the reviewed seed only after their canonical metadata, source URL, scope,
primary label, and at least one pipeline intervention have been checked
against the paper or an official project page. The survey is not a
PRISMA-style systematic review; it is a curated evidence map, and absence
from the catalog must not be interpreted as evidence of irrelevance or
low quality.

\paragraph{Artifact accounting.}
To avoid conflating citation breadth, discovery coverage, and taxonomy
verification, we report three non-interchangeable quantities:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em,style=nextline]
\item[$N_{\mathrm{bib}}$.] The number of entries rendered in the
manuscript bibliography, including surveyed methods, benchmark papers,
foundational components, and auxiliary background works.
\item[$N_{\mathrm{lib}}$.] The number of deduplicated records in the
imported working library. These records retain broad adjacent coverage
and await per-record scope and metadata verification.
\item[$N_{\mathrm{seed}}$.] The number of method records in the released
reviewed catalog seed. Benchmark records and pipeline-intervention
mappings are reported separately because they are different artifact
types rather than additional methods.
\end{description}
The values are pinned to the artifact revision cited in
Appendix~\ref{app:coverage_counts}. The three sets overlap but are not
a monotone screening funnel: a citation need not appear in the imported
working library, and an imported record is not an endorsed taxonomy
placement.

\paragraph{Curation flow.}
New candidates enter a discovery queue, are deduplicated into the
working library, and are promoted to the reviewed seed only after the
record-level checks above. Discovery results are never merged into the
reviewed catalog automatically. The query families, status definitions,
and correction policy are maintained in the companion repository.

\subsection{Coverage Balance}
\label{subsec:coverage_balance}

The final taxonomy is intentionally balanced across historical and
\LLM-era work. Early systems such as \textsc{DrQA}~\cite{chen2017drqa},
\textsc{GRAFT-Net}~\cite{sun2018graftnet}, \textsc{PullNet}~\cite{sun2019pullnet},
\textsc{MDR}~\cite{xiong2021mdr}, \textsc{Baleen}~\cite{khattab2021baleen},

\subsection{Positioning Against Recent Process-Centric Surveys}
\label{subsec:four_axis_position}

Table~\ref{tab:survey_crosswalk} summarizes axis coverage across
recent surveys. Two entries in that table warrant a closer comparison
because they are the nearest neighbours to the present work.

The four-axis framework of Ji et al.~\cite{ji2026retrievalreasoning}
organizes multi-hop retrieval--reasoning systems by execution plan,
index structure, next-step control, and stopping criterion. Its unit
of analysis is procedural: \emph{what the system does at each step.}
Ours is statistical: \emph{which latent quantity the step attempts to
estimate or improve.} The two axes are orthogonal rather than
competing. A process feature such as ``trigger retrieval when
generation uncertainty exceeds a threshold'' can serve different
estimands depending on what the triggered retrieval optimizes: deeper
bridge coverage ($\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}$), selection preservation
($\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}$), or faithfulness verification
($\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}$). Conversely, the same estimand can be
improved by different procedural choices. We therefore adopt the
estimand view as the primary axis
(Sections~\ref{sec:stat_decomposition}
and~\ref{sec:estimand_methods}) while retaining architectural and
procedural positions wherever they help readers trained in prior
taxonomies (Section~\ref{sec:architecture_view}).

The reasoning-oriented \RAG survey of
Liang et al.~\cite{liang2025reasoningragsurvey} is the second closest
neighbour: it covers agentic control and reasoning styles densely and
touches on attribution. It still stops short of a decomposition that
attributes end-to-end success to identifiable latent events, and does
not propose an estimand-aligned reporting standard. We treat this gap,
rather than any incompatibility, as the main opening addressed by
the present survey.

\section{Problem Setup: From Retrieved Documents to Latent Evidence Chains}
\label{sec:problem_setup}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{What Makes a Question Multi-Hop?}
\label{subsec:what_is_mh}

A task is \emph{multi-hop} when its solution requires connecting
multiple evidence units whose discovery or use depends on intermediate
states. Three properties characterize this dependency. First,
\emph{semantic non-locality}: at least one required evidence unit is
not directly retrievable from the original question because its surface
terms are absent. Second, \emph{ordering sensitivity}: evidence must be
combined through an ordered computation, such as bridge resolution,
filtering, projection, comparison, or aggregation. Third, \emph{joint
sufficiency}: no strict subset of the support chain is enough to answer
the question.

These properties distinguish multi-hop \RAG from adjacent tasks. A
long-document task may require high recall but remain single-hop if all
relevant sentences are directly query-relevant. A multi-step generation
task is not multi-hop unless different steps require different external
evidence with cross-step dependency. A hard reasoning task with one
piece of evidence is single-hop with difficult inference, not multi-hop
retrieval. Returning to the running example, the question is multi-hop
because the biography passage stating that Shirley~Temple served as
U.S. Chief of Protocol is not retrievable from the surface terms of
the question about the film \emph{Kiss and Tell}: that biography
becomes queryable only after the bridge entity Shirley~Temple has
been resolved.

\subsection{Latent Chain and Observable Variables}
\label{subsec:latent_chain}

Let $q$ be a question and $\kstore=\{e_1,e_2,\ldots\}$ be a knowledge
store. An evidence unit may be a passage, sentence, span, table cell,
\KG triple, or path. We denote the unobserved support chain required to
answer $q$ by
\begin{equation}
\echain^\star(q)=(e_1^\star,\ldots,e_m^\star),
\end{equation}
where the order encodes dependency among evidence units. The correct
answer is $a^\star$.

A multi-hop \RAG system does not observe $\echain^\star$ directly. It
instead produces a sequence of observable objects:
\begin{align}
L_N &= \retr_N(q), \\
E_K &= S_K(q,L_N), \qquad E_K\subseteq L_N, \quad K\ll N, \\
\pi(E_K) &= \text{an ordering or presentation of } E_K, \\
\hat{a} &= \reason(q,\pi(E_K)).
\end{align}
Here $L_N$ is the retrieved pool, $E_K$ is the selected budgeted
context, $\pi(E_K)$ is the reader-facing ordering, and $\hat{a}$ is the
answer. This sequence defines the observable side of the latent-chain
inference problem. For the running example, $\echain^\star$ has two
ordered units (film-cast passage identifying Shirley~Temple as the
actress who portrayed Corliss~Archer $\to$ biography passage stating
her later role as U.S. Chief of Protocol); $L_N$ might contain the
film-plot summary, several biography passages of other cast members,
distractors about unrelated Shirley~Temple films, and possibly the
answer-bearing biography passage; $E_K$ is the top-$K$ subset that
the selector keeps; $\pi(E_K)$ determines whether the film-cast
passage is placed before or after the biography passage.

\subsection{Failure Modes as Latent-Event Failures}
\label{subsec:failure_modes}

The usual failure modes of multi-hop \RAG can be written as failures of
latent events:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.5em,style=nextline]
\item[Evidence absence.] $\echain^\star\nsubseteq L_N$. A required unit
is missing from the retrieved pool, often at the bridge hop.

\item[Budgeted selection failure.] $\echain^\star\subseteq L_N$ but
$\echain^\star\nsubseteq E_K$. The chain is observable in the pool but
is not preserved under a small context budget.

\item[Exposure failure.] $\echain^\star\subseteq E_K$ but the chain is
placed too late or too diffusely in $\pi(E_K)$ for the reader to use
reliably. This includes lost-in-the-middle~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle} and
ordering-gap effects.

\item[Fusion or reasoning failure.] The chain is selected and visible,
but the reader fails to compose the evidence units into the answer.

\item[Faithfulness failure.] The answer is correct or plausible but is
not causally supported by the retrieved chain. This includes incorrect
citations and parametric leakage.
\end{description}

This event view is stricter than a natural-language taxonomy: it asks
where, in the observable sequence
$L_N\rightarrow E_K\rightarrow \pi(E_K)\rightarrow \hat{a}$, the latent
support chain becomes unavailable, unusable, or non-causal. In the
running example, ``evidence absence'' might be a missing
Shirley~Temple biography passage; ``budgeted selection failure''
might be the biography passage being ranked below high-surface but
unrelated film-plot documents; ``exposure failure'' might be the
answer-bearing biography placed in position eight of an eight-passage
prompt; ``fusion failure'' might be that the reader connects
Shirley~Temple to the wrong later career role; and ``faithfulness
failure'' might be that the reader answers ``U.S. Chief of Protocol''
correctly because that fact is memorized from pretraining, while the
cited chain does not actually entail the answer.

\subsection{Adjacent Problems}
\label{subsec:adjacent}

The latent-chain view also clarifies what is \emph{not} multi-hop
\RAG. Several adjacent research problems share surface machinery with
multi-hop \RAG—retrieval over a corpus, multi-step generation,
compositional reasoning, or grounded question answering—but differ in
whether the target object is a dependency-linked chain of evidence
units. We summarize these distinctions in
Table~\ref{tab:adjacent}. Two axes matter: whether the task's
correctness object is a chain rather than a single fact or a free-form
summary, and whether intermediate steps must be grounded in
retrievable evidence rather than only in model-internal computation.
Adjacent problems typically share one of these axes but not both;
methods designed for them can supply useful components (retrievers,
verifiers, planners) but do not, on their own, address the joint
latent-chain estimation problem that this survey studies.

\subsection{Reasoning Types as Chain Structures}
\label{subsec:reasoning_types_stat}

The latent-chain view gives a compact way to describe common multi-hop
reasoning types. \emph{Bridge reasoning} corresponds to a chain in
which an early evidence unit resolves an unmentioned entity needed to
retrieve a later unit. \emph{Comparison reasoning} corresponds to two
or more parallel subchains whose terminal attributes must be compared.
\emph{Compositional reasoning} corresponds to a sequence of operators
such as filter, project, aggregate, and compare applied over distributed
evidence~\cite{trivedi2022musique,talmor2018complexwebquestions}.
\emph{Verification-oriented reasoning} corresponds to a chain whose
terminal object is not an answer string but a support or refutation
label~\cite{thorne2018fever,jiang2020hover,ma2024exfever}.
\emph{Counterfactual probing} replaces one node or relation in
$\echain^\star$ and tests whether the induced answer distribution
changes~\cite{wu2024cofca}.

These reasoning types differ in the shape of $\echain^\star$. A bridge
question is often close to a path; a comparison question is closer to
a small tree; a fact-verification problem may require a set of jointly
supporting facts; and a table-text question may alternate between
cells, linked passages, and entity mentions~\cite{chen2020hybridqa,
chen2021opentableqa}. The statistical bottlenecks remain the same. The
system must make the relevant structure observable, select it under
budget, expose it to the reader, compose it correctly, and use it
causally. This is why the same estimand-based vocabulary can cover
graph paths, text passages, tables, and agent trajectories.

\subsection{A More Explicit Generative View}
\label{subsec:generative_view}

The preceding notation can be written as a latent-variable model. Let
$\chainset(q)$ denote the set of admissible evidence chains for $q$.
A dataset or world distribution first samples a valid chain
$\echain\in\chainset(q)$, then an answer is generated from the chain:
\begin{align}
\echain &\sim p(\echain\mid q,\kstore),\\
a^\star &\sim p(a\mid q,\echain).
\end{align}
A retrieval-augmented system observes only a corrupted view of this
chain through retrieval and context construction:
\begin{align}
L_N &\sim p_R(L\mid q,\kstore),\\
E_K &\sim p_S(E\mid q,L_N,K),\\
\pi &\sim p_O(\pi\mid q,E_K),\\
\hat a &\sim p_G(a\mid q,\pi(E_K)).
\end{align}
Thus the ideal Bayesian objective is
\begin{equation}
\hat a_{\mathrm{Bayes}}
=\argmax_a \sum_{\echain\in\chainset(q)}p(a\mid q,\echain)
 p(\echain\mid q,\obs_{\mathrm{sys}}),
\end{equation}
where $\obs_{\mathrm{sys}}=(L_N,E_K,\pi(E_K))$ are the observations
made available by the system. Real systems approximate this posterior
through retrieval scores, rerankers, graph traversals, prompts, tool
calls, and verifiers. The statistical question is how each
approximation changes the posterior mass assigned to complete and
causally sufficient chains.

\begin{definition}[Minimal support chain]\label{def:generative_chain}
For a question $q$ and answer $a^\star$, a chain
$\echain=(e_1,\ldots,e_m)$ is \emph{sufficient} if
$p(a^\star\mid q,\echain)$ exceeds a task-specific correctness
threshold. It is \emph{minimal} if no proper subsequence of $\echain$
is sufficient. The set of all minimal chains is denoted by
$\chainset^\star(q)$.
\end{definition}

This definition is deliberately set-valued. Many questions admit
multiple valid chains. A Wikipedia biography, a table row, and a \KG
path may all support the same answer. Treating $\echain^\star$ as a
single sequence is therefore a notational convenience; the more
faithful object is a set or distribution over minimal chains. When the
survey writes $\echain^\star\subseteq L_N$, the statement should be
read as ``there exists at least one valid minimal chain fully contained
in the pool.''

\begin{definition}[Multi-hop dependency graph]
Given a minimal chain $\echain=(e_1,\ldots,e_m)$, define a directed
graph $D_\echain=(V,A)$ whose vertices are evidence units and whose
edge $e_i\rightarrow e_j$ indicates that $e_j$ becomes identifiable,
retrievable, or interpretable only after information in $e_i$ is
known. The hop count is the length of the longest directed path in
$D_\echain$.
\end{definition}

This graph definition clarifies why hop count is not identical to the
number of retrieved passages. A comparison question may have two
parallel single-hop branches but still be multi-hop because the final
comparison operator depends on both branches. A bridge question may
have a path-like structure. A table-text question may alternate
between table cells and linked passages. The chain formalism abstracts
over these substrates.

\begin{assumption}[Budgeted observability]
\label{ass:budgeted_observability}
For a fixed final context budget $K$, a multi-hop system can answer
only if at least one minimal chain has cardinality at most $K$ after
the chosen evidence-unit granularity is fixed.
\end{assumption}

Assumption~\ref{ass:budgeted_observability} is not a statement about
the world; it is a statement about an evaluation protocol. If the
minimal chain requires six passages but the reader receives only five,
then no selector can preserve a passage-level chain. The only possible
remedies are to change the granularity, compress evidence, use a
larger budget, or perform iterative generation with additional
retrieval calls.

\subsection{State-Space and MDP Formulations}
\label{subsec:state_space_mdp}

Many recent agentic and adaptive \RAG methods can be written as a
controlled stochastic process~\cite{asai2024selfrag,jeong2024adaptiverag,
jin2025searchr1,guan2025deeprag,xiong2025raggym}. Let
$s_t=(q,h_t,L_t,M_t)$ be the state at step $t$, where $h_t$ is the
reasoning history, $L_t$ is the accumulated retrieval pool, and $M_t$
is a memory or scratchpad. An action $u_t$ may be a query rewrite,
retrieval call, graph expansion, compression step, verification call,
or final-answer action. The transition is
\begin{equation}
s_{t+1}\sim P(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,u_t,\kstore),
\end{equation}
and the terminal reward can combine correctness, faithfulness, and
cost:
\begin{equation}
R_T = \mathbb{I}\{\hat a=a^\star\}
      -\lambda_c\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\cost(u_t)
      -\lambda_f\mathbb{I}\{\hat a\not\leftarrow \echain^\star\}.
\end{equation}
This casts agentic \RAG as policy learning:
\begin{equation}
\pi^\star=\argmax_\pi
\mathbb{E}_{\pi,P}\left[ R_T \right].
\end{equation}
\textsc{DeepRAG}~\cite{guan2025deeprag},
\textsc{Search-R1}~\cite{jin2025searchr1},
\textsc{R1-Searcher}~\cite{song2025r1searcher},
\textsc{ReSearch}~\cite{chen2025research}, and
\textsc{RAG-Gym}~\cite{xiong2025raggym} differ mainly in what state
variables are observable, how actions are parameterized, whether
rewards are outcome-only or process-supervised, and how retrieved
tokens are handled during optimization.

The MDP view is useful but incomplete. The true support chain is still
latent; the policy receives sparse and biased observations. Therefore,
agentic search does not remove the latent-chain problem. It merely
moves the approximation from a fixed retriever to a learned control
policy over retrieval and reasoning actions.

\subsection{Where Does the Chain Live? Latent Reasoning and Mechanistic
Interpretation}
\label{subsec:latent_mechanism}

A separate but related thread asks whether the chain is even an
explicit object inside modern \LLM-based systems. \emph{Latent
reasoning} methods compress some or all reasoning steps into hidden
states rather than tokens. \textsc{Quiet-STaR}~\cite{goyal2024quietstar}
trains models to silently generate internal rationales before each
token. \textsc{Coconut}~\cite{hao2024coconut} performs continuous
chain-of-thought in a latent space, decoupling reasoning from natural
language. Pause tokens and ``think before speak'' variants insert
non-emitted computation~\cite{goyal2024pause}. From the latent-chain
view, these methods do not eliminate $\echain^\star$; they make it
unobservable through tokens, which complicates both diagnostic
evaluation and faithfulness attribution.

\emph{Mechanistic interpretation} of multi-hop reasoning takes the
opposite stance: it locates traces of $\echain^\star$ inside model
internals. Probing work has shown that \LLMs sometimes recall bridge
entities in intermediate
layers~\cite{geva2023dissecting,yang2024do,biran2024hopping}, that the
recall of a second-hop fact can be circuit-localized~\cite{wang2024grokked},
and that some questions are answered through ``shortcut'' rather than
genuine bridge recall~\cite{yang2024do}. These findings matter for
this survey for two reasons. First, they suggest that even when no
chain is written into the prompt, an implicit chain can sometimes be
recovered from internal activations. Second, they motivate diagnostics
that combine retrieved-evidence sensitivity with internal-state
sensitivity: a model whose answer changes when an internal bridge
representation is patched is more plausibly using the chain causally
than one whose answer is invariant under such interventions.

We return to this dimension in Section~\ref{sec:causal_faithfulness}
when discussing faithfulness diagnostics, and in
Section~\ref{sec:tradeoffs} when discussing open problems for latent
reasoning under retrieval augmentation.

\section{A Statistical Decomposition of Multi-Hop RAG}
\label{sec:stat_decomposition}
% =====================================================================

This section introduces the five statistical quantities used to
organize the rest of the survey. Each quantity corresponds to one
stage at which a latent support chain can be lost or misused.

\subsection{Chain Observability}
\label{subsec:chain_observability}

The first question is whether the complete support chain appears in
the retrieved pool:
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}(q,N)=P(\echain^\star\subseteq L_N\mid q).
\end{equation}
This quantity captures retrieval-side missingness. If
$\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is low, no selector, reader, or verifier can
recover a reliable chain from the pool. When a chain has $m$ hops and
each required unit has marginal retrieval probability $p_i$, then
under an independence approximation,
\begin{equation}
P(\echain^\star\subseteq L_N)\approx \prod_{i=1}^{m}p_i.
\end{equation}
This explains a basic scaling law of multi-hop difficulty: even modest
per-hop miss rates compound rapidly as the chain length grows.
Empirically, on \textsc{MuSiQue}~\cite{trivedi2022musique} 4-hop subsets,
many single-shot retrievers achieve high recall on the first hop but
nearly zero pool-level chain recall, exactly as the product form
predicts.

For the running example, $\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}$ depends on whether
both the \emph{Kiss and Tell} cast passage and the Shirley~Temple
biography passage appear in $L_N$. A single-shot dense retriever
conditioned only on the surface form of the question is drawn toward
film-plot passages and is unlikely to surface the biography of the
bridge entity; the conditional probability of retrieving the
answer-bearing biography rises only after Shirley~Temple has been
resolved as the bridge.

\subsection{Conditional Evidence Utility and Selection Preservation}
\label{subsec:conditional_utility}

Once the chain is observable in $L_N$, two distinct quantities govern
what happens next: (i) a \emph{mechanism} quantity that a selector
uses to rank candidate units against a partial chain, and (ii) an
\emph{event} quantity that records whether the full chain survives
budgeted selection. Conflating the two is a common source of
confusion in the multi-hop literature; we therefore separate them.

\paragraph{Conditional evidence utility (mechanism quantity).}
Standard retrievers estimate marginal relevance $s_{\mathrm{rel}}(q,e)$,
but multi-hop selection requires
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\mathrm{util}}(e\mid q,C)
= P(a^\star\mid q,C\cup\{e\})-P(a^\star\mid q,C),
\end{equation}
which takes values in $[-1,1]$ and measures how much adding $e$ to a
partial context $C$ changes the answer probability. This quantity is
a signed \emph{marginal contribution}; it is not itself a probability.
Bridge documents often look weak under query-only relevance but have
strongly positive conditional utility once a partial chain is known,
and distractors can have negative utility. Reranking, bridge selection,
evidence compression, and core-conditioned selection all approximate
$\theta_{\mathrm{util}}$ or aggregates of it~\cite{khattab2021baleen,
xu2023recomp,yu2023chainofnote,zhang2025parrag}.

\paragraph{Selection preservation (event quantity).}
Whether the selector, guided by any such mechanism, actually keeps the
support chain under a small budget $K$ is a separate probabilistic
event. Given that the chain is present in the pool
($\echain^\star\subseteq L_N$), let
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}
= P\!\left(\echain^\star\subseteq E_K \,\big|\,
\echain^\star\subseteq L_N\right)
\;\in\;[0,1].
\end{equation}
This is the quantity that appears as a factor in the success
decomposition of Section~\ref{subsec:success_factor} and in
Proposition~\ref{prop:bottleneck}. It is a genuine conditional
probability, distinct from the mechanism quantity above. In practice,
$\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}$ is what a Bridge-Recall or Chain-Preservation
metric measures on a held-out set, whereas $\theta_{\mathrm{util}}$ is
what a per-candidate rerank score approximates.

\subsection{Exposure Probability}
\label{subsec:exposure_probability}

Selection is not enough. A reader with limited attention or positional
bias may fail to use a complete chain if the relevant units are late,
scattered, or separated by distractors. We define exposure probability
as
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\mathrm{exp}}(q,K,r)
= P\!\left(\operatorname{rank}_{\pi}(\echain^\star)\le r
\mid \echain^\star\subseteq E_K\right),
\end{equation}
where $\operatorname{rank}_{\pi}(\echain^\star)$ denotes the latest or
most difficult-to-access position of the required chain under ordering
$\pi$, and $r$ is a reader-dependent ``effective access depth'' beyond
which positional bias starts to dominate~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle}.
Ordering, reader-aware reranking, context packing, and compression
methods target this quantity.

\subsection{Fusion Reliability}
\label{subsec:fusion_reliability}

When the chain is present and exposed, the reader must compose it. We
define fusion reliability as
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\mathrm{fus}}(q)
= P(\hat{a}=a^\star\mid
\echain^\star\subseteq E_K,
\operatorname{rank}_{\pi}(\echain^\star)\le r).
\end{equation}
This quantity separates retrieval success from reasoning success. It
explains why high evidence recall does not imply high answer accuracy:
the reader may fail to compare, aggregate, bridge, or verify the
selected evidence.

\subsection{Causal Faithfulness}
\label{subsec:causal_estimand}

Finally, correctness does not imply faithful evidence use. We treat
causal faithfulness as the latent event that the generated answer
actually depends on a valid support chain, rather than merely
co-occurring with plausible evidence or being reconstructed from
parametric memory. Conceptually, let
$\hat a\leftarrow\echain^\star$ denote this causal-grounding event and
define
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}(q)
= P\!\left(\hat a\leftarrow\echain^\star \,\big|\, \hat a=a^\star\right).
\end{equation}
This event is not directly identifiable from answer and citation
outputs alone. We therefore use interventional diagnostics as
observable proxies rather than definitions of faithfulness itself.

One such proxy is chain-deletion sensitivity. Let
$\reason(q,\pi(E_K))$ denote the reader's induced answer distribution.
For any candidate support chain $C\subseteq E_K$, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:faith_intervention}
D_{\mathrm{del}}(C)
=
\Pr\!\left[\reason(q,\pi(E_K))=a^\star\right]
-
\Pr\!\left[\reason(q,\pi(E_K)\setminus C)=a^\star\right].
\end{equation}
For a fixed threshold $\tau>0$, the indicator
$\mathbf{1}\{D_{\mathrm{del}}(C)\ge\tau\}$ is an operational proxy for
whether $C$ is load-bearing for the observed answer. It is neither
necessary nor sufficient for causal faithfulness: deletion can create
out-of-distribution inputs, redundant or alternative valid chains can
make a faithful answer deletion-insensitive, and a shortcut can remain
sensitive to superficial context changes. Accordingly, a credible
faithfulness audit should combine deletion with counterfactual bridge
substitution, citation entailment, and closed-book or conflict controls.

The conceptual faithfulness gap is
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\mathrm{faith}}
= P\!\left(\hat a=a^\star,\; \hat a\not\leftarrow\echain^\star\right),
\end{equation}
but no single deletion statistic is assumed to upper-bound this mass.
Verification, citation checking, counterfactual evaluation, and
process-supervised reward models provide complementary evidence about
this gap~\cite{wu2024cofca,xu2025veritas,lightman2024process,
sun2025rearter,gao2023rarr,gou2024critic,zhao2023verifyedit,
yan2024crag}.

\subsection{Success-Factor Decomposition}
\label{subsec:success_factor}

We define five cumulative latent events along the observable pipeline
$L_N\to E_K\to \pi(E_K)\to \hat a$:
\begin{align}
\obs   &= \{\echain^\star\subseteq L_N\},                                                    \\
\sel   &= \obs\cap\{\echain^\star\subseteq E_K\},                                           \\
\expo  &= \sel\cap\{\operatorname{rank}_{\pi}(\echain^\star)\le r\},                        \\
\fus   &= \expo\cap\{\reason \text{ correctly composes } \echain^\star\},                   \\
\faith &= \fus\cap\{\hat{a}=a^\star \text{ and is causally supported by } \echain^\star\}.
\end{align}
The intersections make the nesting explicit:
$\faith\subseteq\fus\subseteq\expo\subseteq\sel\subseteq\obs$.
We define the
\emph{grounded-success} event as their intersection,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:grounded_success}
Y_g \;=\; \obs\cap\sel\cap\expo\cap\fus\cap\faith,
\end{equation}
and write ordinary answer success as
$Y=\{\hat a=a^\star\}$, so that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:success_split}
P(Y) \;=\; P(Y_g) \;+\; P\!\left(Y\cap \neg Y_g\right),
\end{equation}
where the second term captures shortcuts, parametric leakage, and
lucky guesses in which the answer is correct without a full grounded
chain. Under the nested structure above, the chain rule yields the
\emph{exact} factorization
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:success_decomp}
P(Y_g)
\;=\;
P(\obs)\,P(\sel\mid\obs)\,P(\expo\mid\sel)\,P(\fus\mid\expo)\,P(\faith\mid\fus).
\end{equation}
We denote the five conditional factors by
$\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}=P(\obs)$, $\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}=P(\sel\mid\obs)$,
$\theta_{\mathrm{exp}}=P(\expo\mid\sel)$,
$\theta_{\mathrm{fus}}=P(\fus\mid\expo)$, and
$\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}=P(\faith\mid\fus)$; these are the estimands
whose empirical estimators we survey in Section~\ref{sec:estimand_methods}.
Figure~\ref{fig:bottleneck_estimands} visualizes the conditional
factorization, its active bottleneck, and ordinary answer accuracy.

\paperfigure{0.98}{fig2.pdf}
{Bottleneck visualization of the five estimands. Grounded end-to-end
success $P(Y_g)$ is upper-bounded by the smallest conditional stage factor
across observability, selection preservation, exposure, fusion, and

\begin{remark}[On the meaning of equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp}]
Equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp} is an exact chain-rule decomposition
of the grounded-success event $Y_g$ and does not assume conditional
independence. It is a \emph{diagnostic} factorization in the sense
that each conditional factor is empirically estimable on the subset
of instances that reach the preceding event: $\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}$
on instances with $\obs$, $\theta_{\mathrm{exp}}$ on instances with
$\sel$, and so on. Because real systems admit shortcut and
parametric-leakage events, $P(Y)$ can exceed $P(Y_g)$; the gap
$P(Y)-P(Y_g)$ is itself a diagnostic quantity and is what
faithfulness-oriented evaluation (Section~\ref{sec:causal_faithfulness})
seeks to expose.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Conditional bottleneck bound]
\label{prop:bottleneck}
Let $\theta_i$ denote the five conditional stage factors in
equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp}. Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:min_event_bound}
P(Y_g)=\prod_i\theta_i \;\le\; \min_i \theta_i.
\end{equation}
If the minimum factor is positive, equality requires every other factor
to equal one; if any factor is zero, both sides are zero. Thus grounded
success cannot exceed the weakest conditional stage-survival rate.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Every $\theta_i$ lies in $[0,1]$. For any index $k$,
$\prod_i\theta_i=\theta_k\prod_{j\ne k}\theta_j\le\theta_k$;
taking the minimum over $k$ proves the bound. The equality conditions
follow directly from the product of the remaining factors.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{A coordinate view of local sensitivity.}
The product of the five conditional factors is exactly $P(Y_g)$ by
equation~\eqref{eq:success_decomp}. What is heuristic is treating
those factors as independently perturbable coordinates. Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surrogate_product}
\Phi(\boldsymbol\theta)
\;\coloneqq\;
\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}\,\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}\,\theta_{\mathrm{exp}}\,
\theta_{\mathrm{fus}}\,\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}
\;=\;P(Y_g).
\end{equation}
The coordinate derivative
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surrogate_sensitivity}
\frac{\partial\Phi}{\partial\theta_i}
\;=\;\prod_{j\ne i}\theta_j
\end{equation}
describes the local change in the product if one factor could be varied
while the others were held fixed. The product identity is exact for
the conditional factors; only the interpretation of an isolated
partial derivative as an achievable system intervention is heuristic,
because in real systems changing a retriever, selector, or reader
generally changes several conditional distributions at once. We
therefore use equation~\eqref{eq:surrogate_sensitivity} only to
summarize coordinate sensitivity, not to bound the true marginal gain
of a system update.

Proposition~\ref{prop:bottleneck} summarizes a direct implication of
the conditional factorization: a strong retriever does not improve
grounded accuracy when the reader cannot fuse evidence; a strong
reader does not help when the chain is absent from the pool; a
faithfulness verifier may reject unfaithful answers but cannot
recover missing evidence. It also implies that the most credible
empirical papers are those that show \emph{where in the product} the
gain occurs, and that headline gains in $P(Y)$ without a matching
gain in the corresponding component event are candidates for
shortcut-driven improvement rather than grounded improvement.

\subsection{Risk under Missingness, Noise, and Budget}
\label{subsec:risk_view}

The estimand view also yields a compact risk perspective. Let
$\rho_N$ and $\rho_K$ denote, respectively, the noise ratios of the
retrieved pool and the selected context,
\begin{equation}
\rho_N = \frac{|L_N\setminus \echain^\star|}{|L_N|},
\qquad
\rho_K = \frac{|E_K\setminus \echain^\star|}{|E_K|},
\end{equation}
so that $\rho_N$ measures pool-level distractor mass and $\rho_K$
measures how much of the budgeted context is spent on non-chain
units. A stylized system risk can be written as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}(N,K)
= \alpha\, P(\echain^\star\nsubseteq L_N)
+ \beta\, \mathbb{E}[\rho_K]
+ \gamma\, \operatorname{Cost}(N,K)
+ \delta\, \Delta_{\mathrm{faith}},
\end{equation}
where the expectation in the second term is over questions and
retrieval outcomes.
This expression makes explicit why multi-hop \RAG is not solved by
``retrieve more'' or ``use a larger reader.'' Deeper retrieval reduces
missingness but increases noise and cost; smaller contexts reduce noise
but increase selection bias; stronger readers may improve fusion while
leaving faithfulness unresolved.

\subsection{Identifiability and Surrogate Supervision}
\label{subsec:identifiability}

The five estimands are conceptually clean but not always directly
observable. In most datasets, $\echain^\star$ is partially annotated,
non-unique, or absent. Supporting-fact labels are only a surrogate for
chain membership; they may omit alternative valid chains, include
facts that are sufficient but not necessary, or differ from the path
actually used by the model. This creates an identifiability problem:
answer accuracy can be observed, but the latent event that failed is
often not identified without additional instrumentation.

\begin{proposition}[Conditional identifiability]
\label{prop:identifiability}
Suppose $\chainset^\star(q)$ is the full set of valid support chains
for question $q$, and that for each example we observe a subset
$\Sigma(q)\subseteq\chainset^\star(q)$ of annotated valid chains and
the full observable sequence $(L_N,E_K,\pi(E_K),\hat a)$. Define the
annotated versions of the observability, selection, and exposure
events by
\begin{align}
\obs^{\Sigma}   &= \{\exists C\in\Sigma(q):\, C\subseteq L_N\},                          \\
\sel^{\Sigma}   &= \{\exists C\in\Sigma(q):\, C\subseteq E_K\},                          \\
\expo^{\Sigma}  &= \{\exists C\in\Sigma(q):\, C\subseteq E_K\text{ and }
                    \operatorname{rank}_{\pi}(C)\le r\}.
\end{align}
Then $\obs^{\Sigma}$, $\sel^{\Sigma}$, and $\expo^{\Sigma}$ are
directly identifiable from $(\Sigma(q),L_N,E_K,\pi(E_K))$ and satisfy
$\obs^{\Sigma}\subseteq\obs$, $\sel^{\Sigma}\subseteq\sel$,
$\expo^{\Sigma}\subseteq\expo$; an observed positive on the
annotated event implies the underlying latent event, while an
observed negative on the annotated event is informative for the
latent event only up to the chain-set gap
$\chainset^\star(q)\setminus\Sigma(q)$. The events $\fus$ and
$\faith$ are not identifiable from
$(\Sigma(q),L_N,E_K,\pi(E_K),\hat a)$ alone; they require
interventional probes such as evidence deletion, bridge
counterfactual, or oracle-evidence comparison
(Section~\ref{sec:causal_faithfulness}).
\end{proposition}

We distinguish three supervision regimes. In \emph{fully annotated}
regimes, path or step labels~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa,ho2020wikimultihopqa,
trivedi2022musique,dalvi2021entailmentbank} allow approximate
measurement of observability and selection. In \emph{weakly annotated}
regimes, supporting facts or citations provide noisy labels for
membership and faithfulness. In \emph{unannotated} regimes, the system
must rely on proxies such as retrieval overlap, counterfactual
perturbation, leave-one-out deletion~\cite{liu2023evaluating}, or
verifier scores~\cite{xu2025veritas}.

These regimes should not be mixed without qualification. A method that
improves answer F1 under unannotated evaluation may have improved
observability, fusion, or parametric guessing; without estimand-level
diagnostics the mechanism is underdetermined. This perspective
suggests a reporting standard for future work
(Section~\ref{sec:reporting_guidelines}): for any multi-hop system,
authors should report at least one metric for each of three stages:
pool-level chain coverage, context-level chain preservation, and
answer-level faithfulness.

\subsection{A Diagnostic Protocol}
\label{subsec:diagnostic_protocol}

The statistical decomposition induces a simple diagnostic protocol.
First, evaluate the retrieved pool $L_N$ against available support
labels to estimate chain observability. If the chain is absent,
downstream reader errors should not be attributed to reasoning.
Second, condition on chain-present examples and measure whether the
selected budgeted context $E_K$ preserves the chain. This isolates
selection bias under budget. Third, hold membership fixed and vary the
ordering $\pi(E_K)$ to measure exposure effects. Fourth, evaluate the
reader under oracle or near-oracle evidence to estimate fusion
reliability. Fifth, perturb or remove cited evidence to test causal
faithfulness.

The important point is that these diagnostics are conditional. A
faithfulness test on examples where the evidence is absent conflates
retrieval and attribution. A fusion test on examples where the chain
is not selected conflates selection and reasoning. A ranking test that
changes both membership and order cannot isolate exposure. Conditional
evaluation is therefore not a minor experimental detail; it is
required by the latent-chain factorization itself.

\subsection{Monotonicity, Interaction, and Submodularity}
\label{subsec:submodularity}

A common implicit assumption in retrieval pipelines is that evidence
utility is approximately additive: if each document is relevant on its
own, the set should be useful. Multi-hop reasoning violates this
assumption. Let $F_q(E)$ denote the probability that a fixed reader
answers correctly given evidence set $E$:
\begin{equation}
F_q(E)=P(\hat a=a^\star\mid q,E).
\end{equation}
The marginal utility of an evidence unit $e$ under current context $C$
is
\begin{equation}
\Delta_q(e\mid C)=F_q(C\cup\{e\})-F_q(C).
\end{equation}
A single-hop task is often close to monotone submodular selection:
adding a relevant document helps and marginal gains diminish.
Multi-hop tasks are frequently non-submodular because a bridge
document may have near-zero utility alone but large utility after its
companion evidence is present.

\begin{definition}[Complementarity gap]
For evidence units $e_i,e_j$ and context $C$, define
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_q(e_i,e_j\mid C)
= \Delta_q(e_j\mid C\cup\{e_i\})-\Delta_q(e_j\mid C).
\end{equation}
Positive $\Gamma_q$ indicates complementarity: the value of $e_j$
increases after $e_i$ is known.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Why query-only relevance misses bridges]
\label{prop:bridge_miss}
Suppose a bridge evidence unit $e_b$ has low marginal relevance
$s_{\mathrm{rel}}(q,e_b)$ but positive complementarity
$\Gamma_q(e_c,e_b\mid C)>0$ with a core unit $e_c$. Any selector that
is monotone in $s_{\mathrm{rel}}(q,e)$ alone can rank $e_b$ below
unrelated high-surface-match distractors even when
$\Delta_q(e_b\mid C\cup\{e_c\})$ is high.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof]
A query-only selector is a function of $s_{\mathrm{rel}}(q,e)$ and does
not condition on $C\cup\{e_c\}$. If there exists a distractor $d$ such
that $s_{\mathrm{rel}}(q,d)>s_{\mathrm{rel}}(q,e_b)$, the selector will
prefer $d$ regardless of the conditional gain of $e_b$. The inequality
can hold even when $F_q(C\cup\{e_c,e_b\})>F_q(C\cup\{e_c,d\})$, because
that comparison depends on conditional utility rather than marginal
surface relevance. The argument requires only that the selector's
score function depends on $(q,e)$ and not on $C$, and that the
strict-inequality regime is non-empty---a condition that is empirically
verified on bridge-heavy benchmarks such as
\textsc{MuSiQue}~\cite{trivedi2022musique} and
\textsc{2WikiMultiHopQA}~\cite{ho2020wikimultihopqa}.
\end{proof}

This proposition is the formal reason why multi-hop reranking should
not be evaluated only as relevance reranking. A selector may reduce
average query-document relevance while improving chain preservation.
Conversely, a strong cross-encoder reranker may improve top-$K$
relevance while still dropping low-surface bridge evidence.

\subsection{A Budgeted Chain-Preservation Objective}
\label{subsec:budgeted_objective}

Given a pool $L_N$, context budget $K$, and cost function $c(e)$, an
ideal selector solves
\begin{equation}
E_K^\star
=\argmax_{E\subseteq L_N}
\mathbb{E}_{\echain\sim p(\echain\mid q,L_N)}
\left[\mathbb{I}\{\echain\subseteq E\}\right]
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad |E|\le K.
\end{equation}
If evidence units have variable length, the cardinality budget becomes
a token budget:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{e\in E} c(e)\le B.
\end{equation}
Compression methods can be interpreted as changing $c(e)$ and sometimes
changing the evidence unit itself. A compressed note may preserve the
answer-bearing fact but lose the bridge relation or the provenance
needed for faithfulness. Therefore compression should be evaluated not
only by answer accuracy but by chain preservation and citation
validity~\cite{xu2023recomp,yu2023chainofnote}.

\begin{lemma}[Upper bound under chain absence]
\label{lem:absence_upper_bound}
Let $A$ be the event that no valid minimal chain is contained in the
retrieved pool $L_N$. If the reader is required to answer only from
the retrieved evidence and has no parametric leakage, then
\begin{equation}
P(\hat a=a^\star\mid A)\le \epsilon,
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon$ is the probability of guessing correctly from task
priors or annotation artifacts.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Under the no-leakage condition, the answer is identifiable only from
a sufficient evidence chain. When no valid minimal chain is present,
the reader lacks the information required to distinguish $a^\star$
from alternatives except through prior bias. The remaining success
probability is therefore bounded by shortcut or guessing probability
$\epsilon$. Formally, let $\Pi(a\mid A)$ be the conditional answer
distribution when no chain is present. Then for any reader output
$\hat a$, $P(\hat a=a^\star\mid A)=\Pi(a^\star\mid A)\le\max_{a}
\Pi(a\mid A)\le\epsilon$ by the assumption on prior coverage.
\end{proof}

The lemma motivates conditional evaluation. If a method fails on
examples where $L_N$ lacks the chain, the failure should be attributed
to observability, not to the reader. If a method succeeds in that
regime, the success may indicate parametric memory or shortcut
exploitation rather than retrieval-grounded reasoning.

\subsection{Exposure as a Positional Treatment}
\label{subsec:exposure_treatment}

Exposure can be treated as a causal treatment over a fixed evidence
set. Let $Y(\pi)$ denote the answer-correctness outcome under
ordering $\pi$. The average exposure effect between two ordering
policies $\pi_1$ and $\pi_0$ is
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\mathrm{exp}}
=\mathbb{E}\left[Y(\pi_1)-Y(\pi_0)\mid \echain^\star\subseteq E_K\right].
\end{equation}
This estimand is identifiable only if membership is held fixed. If
the reranker changes both $E_K$ and $\pi(E_K)$, gains conflate
selection and presentation. This is why fixed-membership ordering
experiments are not secondary ablations; they are required to isolate
the exposure channel, especially on long contexts where positional
bias is documented to be substantial~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle,
yen2024lcontext,bai2024longbench}.

\subsection{Reader Fusion as Noisy Program Execution}
\label{subsec:fusion_program}

A multi-hop reader can be idealized as executing a latent program over
evidence units:
\begin{equation}
z_0=q,\qquad z_t = T_t(z_{t-1},e_t),\qquad \hat a=H(z_m),
\end{equation}
where $T_t$ may resolve an entity, project an attribute, compare
values, or aggregate partial results. If each transition has error
probability $\epsilon_t$, then under a simple independent-error
approximation,
\begin{equation}
P(\text{all transitions correct})\approx \prod_{t=1}^m(1-\epsilon_t).
\end{equation}
This explains why longer chains are difficult even when every document
is retrieved. It also explains why process
supervision~\cite{lightman2024process,sun2025rearter,xiong2025raggym},
program execution~\cite{chen2023pot,lyu2023faithfulcot,gao2023pal},
external tools~\cite{yao2023react,schick2023toolformer}, and
step-level verification can improve multi-hop \RAG without changing
retrieval recall.

\section{Methods as Estimators of Evidence-Chain Quantities}
\label{sec:estimand_methods}
% =====================================================================

This section reorganizes the method landscape by statistical estimand.
Architectural families reappear, but as implementation choices rather
than primary organizing categories. The subsections below deliberately
cut across architecture. For example, graph expansion, iterative dense
retrieval, and agentic search all appear under chain observability
because they expand or adapt the set of candidate evidence units.
Decomposition appears under fusion reliability when it reduces the
complexity of composition, but it also appears under observability when
sub-questions become retrieval queries. This overlap is not a flaw; it
is the point of an estimand-based taxonomy. A method can be an
estimator of more than one quantity, and a strong system often combines
several estimators. Readers who prefer an architecture-first
presentation should read this section together with
Section~\ref{sec:architecture_view}, which catalogues the same methods
under their conventional family labels.

\subsection{Estimating Chain Observability}
\label{subsec:methods_observability}

Methods in this group aim to increase $P(\echain^\star\subseteq L_N)$.
We organize them along increasing degrees of adaptivity: single-shot
retrieval, iterative retrieval, graph expansion, and agentic search.

\subsubsection{Single-shot Sparse and Dense Retrieval}
Sparse retrievers such as BM25~\cite{robertson2009bm25} and tuned BM25
variants remain strong baselines for multi-hop \QA when at least one
hop is lexically anchored. Dense retrievers including
\textsc{DPR}~\cite{karpukhin2020dpr}, \textsc{REALM}~\cite{guu2020realm},
\textsc{ANCE}~\cite{xiong2020ance},
\textsc{Contriever}~\cite{izacard2022contriever},
\textsc{GTR}~\cite{ni2022gtr}, \textsc{E5}~\cite{wang2022e5},
\textsc{BGE}~\cite{xiao2024bge}, and
\textsc{RetroMAE}~\cite{xiao2022retromae} provide scalable proposal
pools by encoding question and passage into a shared dense space.
Multi-vector and late-interaction retrievers such as
\textsc{ColBERT}~\cite{khattab2020colbert} and
\textsc{ColBERTv2}~\cite{santhanam2022colbertv2} preserve per-token
matching signals that are useful when bridge entities appear only in
narrow spans. Learned sparse retrievers such as
\textsc{SPLADE}~\cite{formal2021splade} combine inverted-index
efficiency with neural expansion. The shared limitation of single-shot
retrievers in the multi-hop setting is structural: if the second-hop
bridge is not expressed in the original query, query-only retrieval may
miss it, regardless of whether scoring is sparse, dense, or multi-vector.

\subsubsection{Iterative and Reasoning-Interleaved Retrieval}
Iterative retrieval directly addresses second-hop missingness.
\textsc{GoldEn Retriever}~\cite{min2019golden} generates intermediate
queries from previously retrieved passages.
\textsc{MDR}~\cite{xiong2021mdr} performs multi-step dense retrieval
conditioned on accumulated evidence representations.
\textsc{Baleen}~\cite{khattab2021baleen} maintains a condensed evidence
state across hops to avoid query drift.
\textsc{MUPPET}~\cite{feldman2019muppet} pre-encodes potential bridge
links and reuses them between hops.
\textsc{IRCoT}~\cite{trivedi2023ircot} interleaves chain-of-thought
reasoning with retrieval, treating each reasoning step as a new query.
\textsc{FLARE}~\cite{jiang2023flare} triggers retrieval mid-generation
whenever predicted-token probability falls below a threshold.
\textsc{ITER-RETGEN}~\cite{shao2023iterretgen} alternates between
generation-as-context-expansion and retrieval-as-evidence-refresh.
\textsc{Adaptive-RAG}~\cite{jeong2024adaptiverag} classifies query
complexity to select between no-retrieval, single-step, and multi-step
retrieval paths. \textsc{DRAGIN}~\cite{su2024dragin} decides retrieval
timing based on generator entropy and uncertainty signals.
\textsc{Self-RAG}~\cite{asai2024selfrag} uses learned reflection tokens
to decide both when to retrieve and which passages to keep.
\textsc{ITER-DRAGON}~\cite{wu2024iterdragon} composes iterative
retrieval with stage-wise verifier feedback.

\subsubsection{Graph Expansion as Observability Estimator}
Graph-expansion methods can also be read as observability estimators.
\textsc{GRAFT-Net}~\cite{sun2018graftnet} and
\textsc{PullNet}~\cite{sun2019pullnet} construct question-specific
subgraphs by iteratively pulling neighboring entities and linked text.
\textsc{HGN}~\cite{fang2020hgn} and
\textsc{DFGN}~\cite{qiu2019dfgn} build hierarchical entity-and-paragraph
graphs to surface bridge nodes that single-shot retrieval misses.
\textsc{ToG}~\cite{sun2024tog}, \textsc{ToG 2.0}~\cite{ma2024tog2}, and
\textsc{RoG}~\cite{luo2024rog} traverse \KG paths under \LLM control,
expanding the local search space until candidate paths become visible.
\textsc{KG-FiD}~\cite{yu2022kgfid} uses \KG signals to prune and
re-score retrieved passages. \textsc{GNN-RAG}~\cite{mavromatis2024gnnrag}
runs a \GNN over a retrieved subgraph and feeds the resulting node
representations to an \LLM reasoner.
\textsc{GraphRAG}~\cite{edge2024graphrag} constructs an \LLM-built
graph index over a corpus and uses community summaries for
query-focused expansion. \textsc{LightRAG}~\cite{guo2024lightrag} and
\textsc{HippoRAG}~\cite{gutierrez2024hipporag} provide alternative
graph indexing strategies optimized for cost and memory-style
retrieval.

\subsubsection{Long-context and Hierarchical Retrieval}
A different way to increase chain observability is to change the
granularity of evidence units. Long-context readers can sometimes
absorb the entire candidate pool, but lost-in-the-middle
effects~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle} prevent naive scaling.
\textsc{RAPTOR}~\cite{sarthi2024raptor} builds recursive abstractive
summaries of corpus clusters so that high-level bridge information
becomes retrievable.
\textsc{LongRAG}~\cite{jiang2024longrag} pairs long-context readers
with coarser retrieval units to reduce per-hop selection load.
\textsc{HiQA}~\cite{chen2024hiqa} introduces hierarchical context
augmentation. \textsc{HyDE}~\cite{gao2023hyde} synthesizes
hypothetical documents to anchor dense retrieval in semantic rather
than lexical space, and is especially useful for queries whose
surface form does not match relevant evidence.

\subsubsection{Agentic and RL-trained Search Policies}
Recent work moves observability estimation into a learned policy.
\textsc{ReAct}~\cite{yao2023react} interleaves reasoning and acting
through tool calls including search.
\textsc{Toolformer}~\cite{schick2023toolformer} learns when to invoke
external tools through self-supervision.
\textsc{Search-R1}~\cite{jin2025searchr1} trains a reasoning model to
generate search queries during step-by-step reasoning with
reinforcement learning, with retrieval results masked out from policy
gradients. \textsc{R1-Searcher}~\cite{song2025r1searcher} incentivizes
the search capability of \LLMs through RL with intermediate retrieval
rewards. \textsc{DeepRAG}~\cite{guan2025deeprag} formulates
retrieval-augmented reasoning as an MDP and decides at each step
whether to retrieve or rely on parametric reasoning.
\textsc{RAG-Gym}~\cite{xiong2025raggym} adds process supervision for
search agents, making intermediate search actions trainable.
\textsc{PAR-RAG}~\cite{zhang2025parrag} uses a plan-act-review structure
to reduce reasoning-path drift.
\textsc{DualRAG}~\cite{cheng2025dualrag} couples reasoning-augmented
querying with progressive knowledge aggregation.
\textsc{MultiCube-RAG}~\cite{shi2026multicuberag} uses an
ontology-guided cube structure to organize multi-hop retrieval
without relying solely on noisy graph expansion.
\textsc{ReSearch}~\cite{chen2025research} learns to reason with search
via reinforcement learning under sparse outcome rewards.
\textsc{O1-Searcher}~\cite{wang2025o1searcher} and similar systems
explore deliberate search in a long-thought regime. Their advantage is
adaptive depth; their risk is query drift, redundant calls, and weak
attribution of which action improved observability.

\subsection{Estimating Selection Preservation}
\label{subsec:methods_utility}

A chain can be present in $L_N$ but absent from $E_K$ because the
selection model optimizes marginal relevance rather than conditional
utility. Rerankers, bridge selectors, compression methods, and
plan-guided selectors all aim to preserve the support chain under a
small budget; their common internal mechanism is to approximate the
conditional utility score $\theta_{\mathrm{util}}(e\mid q,C)$, which
serves as a per-candidate signal in service of the event-level
estimand $\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}$.

\subsubsection{Cross-encoder and \LLM Rerankers}
Classical rerankers~\cite{nogueira2019passage} and stronger neural
rerankers including \textsc{monoT5}~\cite{nogueira2020monot5},
\textsc{RankT5}~\cite{zhuang2023rankt5},
\textsc{RankLLaMA}~\cite{ma2024rankllama},
\textsc{RankGPT}~\cite{sun2023rankgpt},
\textsc{RankZephyr}~\cite{pradeep2023rankzephyr}, and listwise
\LLM rerankers~\cite{tang2024found} score candidate documents by query
relevance. In multi-hop settings, these scores are often
misspecified: bridge documents can have low standalone relevance but
high value after a partial chain has been observed. Some recent
listwise \LLM rerankers attempt joint scoring and are therefore closer
to conditional utility estimation, but the gap from true conditional
utility is rarely measured.

\subsubsection{Bridge-aware and Core-conditioned Selection}
Methods that condition selection on partial state begin to estimate
true conditional utility. \textsc{Baleen}'s condensed
retrieval~\cite{khattab2021baleen} explicitly tracks accumulated
evidence and reranks under that condition. \textsc{HopRetriever}
\cite{li2020hopretriever} and \textsc{PathRetriever}~\cite{asai2020learning}
score candidate next-hop documents conditioned on entity or sentence
state. \textsc{BeamDR}~\cite{zhao2021beamdr} performs beam-search over
chain hypotheses. \textsc{MuGER2}~\cite{yang2022muger} explicitly
detects bridge entities. \textsc{Bridge-R}~\cite{xu2024bridger} adds a
bridge-prediction objective to dense retrieval.

\subsubsection{Compression and Note-based Methods}
Compression can be interpreted as utility estimation under a token
budget. \textsc{LongLLMLingua}~\cite{jiang2023llmlingua}, and
\textsc{LongLLMLingua-2}~\cite{pan2024longllmlingua2} compress prompts
by predicting token importance. \textsc{RECOMP}~\cite{xu2023recomp}
compresses retrieved passages into extractive or abstractive notes.
\textsc{Chain-of-Note}~\cite{yu2023chainofnote} writes intermediate
notes that survive into the reader prompt. \textsc{xRAG}~\cite{cheng2024xrag}
compresses retrieved context into single tokens for efficient
augmentation. The key statistical question for compression is whether
chain-relevant complementary content survives, not whether the
compressor preserves average relevance.

\subsubsection{Plan-driven and Sub-question Selection}
Sub-question and plan-based methods select evidence aligned with
explicit reasoning steps. \textsc{DecompRC}~\cite{min2019decomprc},
\textsc{Self-Ask}~\cite{press2022selfask},
\textsc{Least-to-Most}~\cite{zhou2023leasttomost},
\textsc{BREAK}~\cite{wolfson2020break},
\textsc{Plan-and-Solve}~\cite{wang2023planandsolve},
\textsc{HiPRAG}~\cite{vu2025hiprag},
\textsc{ProgramFC}~\cite{pan2023programfc}, and \textsc{PAR-RAG}'s
plan stage all transform a complex question into sub-tasks whose
matched evidence is more nearly single-hop. The estimand interpretation
is that decomposition reduces the variance of utility estimation: a
sub-question constrains conditional context $C$ explicitly, so each
candidate document can be scored under a clearer conditional.

\subsubsection{Training Signals for Utility}
Conditional utility can be supervised in several ways: gold supporting
facts~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa,trivedi2022musique}; oracle ablations
producing leave-one-out labels; reader-derived utility scores;
verifier scores~\cite{xu2025veritas,sun2025rearter}; and counterfactual
deletion~\cite{liu2023evaluating}. Each signal has bias. Gold supports
may not be unique. Reader-derived utility can inherit reader-specific
preferences. Verifier scores may reward plausibility rather than
causal support. A robust selector should ideally combine multiple
signals while reporting which utility surrogate is used.

From this view, the central statistical issue is selection bias under
budget. A top-$K$ context is not a neutral sample of the retrieved
pool; it is a biased estimator of the latent support chain. Future
selection models should therefore report not only relevance metrics
but also conditional chain preservation: whether the missing supports
of $\echain^\star$ survive the transition from $L_N$ to $E_K$.

\subsection{Estimating Exposure Probability}
\label{subsec:methods_exposure}

Exposure methods assume the relevant evidence is selected but not
necessarily usable. Long contexts and multi-document prompts create
position-dependent access problems~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle,
yen2024lcontext}.

\subsubsection{Reader-aware Ordering and Packing}
Reader-aware ordering places bridge and answer-bearing evidence
earlier or closer together. \textsc{LongChat-aware} packing strategies,
``lost-in-the-middle'' mitigations~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle},
\textsc{LongContextBench}-driven layout heuristics~\cite{bai2024longbench},
and explicit instruction templates that announce chain structure are
examples of low-cost ordering techniques. Some methods learn the
ordering: \textsc{PRCA}~\cite{yang2023prca} trains a context-aware
adapter that re-orders evidence based on reader gradients.
\textsc{Position-Robust Context}~\cite{he2024positionrobust} fine-tunes
\LLMs to be position-invariant.

\subsubsection{Fusion Architecture as Exposure}
Architectures that decouple encoding from fusion reduce
encoder-side interference. \textsc{FiD}~\cite{izacard2021fid}
separately encodes passages and fuses them in the decoder, making each
passage's contribution position-independent in the encoder.
\textsc{FiD-Light}~\cite{hofstatter2023fidlight} and
\textsc{KG-FiD}~\cite{yu2022kgfid} extend the same idea with
efficiency or structural priors. \textsc{Atlas}~\cite{izacard2022atlas}
co-trains retriever and \FiD reader. Encoder-decoder separation is
itself an exposure intervention because the reader no longer faces a
single concatenated long prompt.

\subsubsection{Compression-aware Exposure}
Compression reduces exposure competition by shortening distractors.
\textsc{LLMLingua}~\cite{jiang2023llmlingua},
\textsc{RECOMP}~\cite{xu2023recomp}, and
\textsc{Selective-Context}~\cite{li2023selective} all shrink the input
and indirectly improve the reader's effective access depth. In fixed
membership experiments, compression can isolate the exposure channel
because it changes neither $\sel$ nor the underlying retrieval.

\subsubsection{Fixed-Membership Ordering as a Causal Probe}
A method can preserve evidence membership while still improving answer
quality by changing presentation. Fixed-membership ordering ablations
where only the order of $E_K$ varies, holding $|E_K|$ and contents
constant, isolate $\theta_{\mathrm{exp}}$. Section~\ref{sec:reporting_guidelines}
recommends this experiment as a standard supplement.

\subsection{Estimating Fusion Reliability}
\label{subsec:methods_fusion}

Fusion reliability asks whether the reader can compose the chain once
it is selected and exposed.

\subsubsection{Retrieve-then-Read Readers}
Retrieve-then-read systems such as \textsc{DrQA}~\cite{chen2017drqa},
\textsc{ORQA}~\cite{lee2019orqa}, \textsc{RAG}~\cite{lewis2020rag},
\textsc{REALM}~\cite{guu2020realm}, \textsc{Atlas}~\cite{izacard2022atlas},
and \textsc{FiD}~\cite{izacard2021fid} are direct fusion estimators.
They differ in whether retrieval is fixed, jointly trained, or
distilled, and in whether passages are concatenated or independently
encoded. Their shared role is to estimate $P(\hat a\mid q,E)$.

\subsubsection{Graph-aware Fusion}
Graph-aware fusion methods such as
\textsc{QA-GNN}~\cite{yasunaga2021qagnn},
\textsc{GreaseLM}~\cite{zhang2022greaselm},
\textsc{UniKGQA}~\cite{jiang2023unikgqa},
\textsc{ReaRev}~\cite{mavromatis2022rearev},
\textsc{SR}~\cite{zhang2022subgraph},
\textsc{HamQA}~\cite{dong2023hierarchical},
and \textsc{NSM}~\cite{he2021nsm} provide structured inductive bias
for cross-evidence composition. The graph constrains how evidence
units can be combined and therefore reduces the hypothesis space the
reader must search over.

\subsubsection{Decomposition and Programmatic Reasoning}
Decomposition-based methods factorize a difficult multi-hop query into
easier subproblems. \textsc{DecompRC}~\cite{min2019decomprc},
\textsc{Self-Ask}~\cite{press2022selfask},
\textsc{Least-to-Most}~\cite{zhou2023leasttomost},
\textsc{BREAK}~\cite{wolfson2020break},
\textsc{Plan-and-Solve}~\cite{wang2023planandsolve},
\textsc{Decomposed Prompting}~\cite{khot2023decomposed},
\textsc{Successive Prompting}~\cite{dua2022successive}, and
\textsc{ReasonPath}~\cite{li2023reasonpath} all reduce fusion
complexity by making intermediate operations explicit. Programmatic
methods such as \textsc{PoT}~\cite{chen2023pot},
\textsc{PAL}~\cite{gao2023pal},
\textsc{Faithful CoT}~\cite{lyu2023faithfulcot}, and
\textsc{Code-as-Reasoning}~\cite{wang2024executable} make intermediate
operations executable or symbolically checkable. The main fusion-side
failure is error propagation: a wrong early sub-answer corrupts later
retrieval and reasoning, an effect amplified by the
$\prod(1-\epsilon_t)$ form of equation in \S\ref{subsec:fusion_program}.

\subsubsection{Self-Consistency and Diverse Reasoning}
Sampling-based methods improve fusion through diversity.
\textsc{Self-Consistency}~\cite{wang2023selfconsistency} marginalizes
over multiple reasoning traces. \textsc{Tree-of-Thought}~\cite{yao2023tot}
and \textsc{Graph-of-Thought}~\cite{besta2024got} expand the reasoning
topology beyond linear chains.
\textsc{Algorithm-of-Thought}~\cite{sel2024algorithm} introduces
algorithmic templates. \textsc{Auto-CoT}~\cite{zhang2023autocot}
automatically constructs in-context exemplars. These methods do not
change retrieval but increase the probability that at least one
trajectory composes the chain correctly.

\subsubsection{Latent and Compressed Reasoning Inside Readers}
A separate fusion thread compresses reasoning into hidden states.
\textsc{Quiet-STaR}~\cite{goyal2024quietstar},
\textsc{Coconut}~\cite{hao2024coconut},
pause-token methods~\cite{goyal2024pause}, and ``think before speak''
variants generate internal computation that is not emitted as tokens.
In the latent-chain view, these methods do not eliminate fusion error;
they relocate it to model internals where it is harder to attribute.
Empirically they can improve multi-hop accuracy on chain-explicit
tasks~\cite{wang2024grokked} while making external diagnostics less
informative.

\subsection{Estimating Causal Faithfulness}
\label{subsec:methods_faithfulness}

Faithfulness methods estimate whether the answer is causally grounded
in evidence rather than merely correct.

\subsubsection{Self-verification}
Self-verification methods use model-internal consistency or critique.
\textsc{Self-Refine}~\cite{madaan2023selfrefine},
\textsc{Reflexion}~\cite{shinn2023reflexion},
\textsc{CoVe}~\cite{dhuliawala2024cove},
\textsc{SelfCheckGPT}~\cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt}, and
\textsc{Self-Verification}~\cite{weng2023selfverification} score
intermediate or final outputs by re-asking the model. They are cheap
but bounded by the model's own knowledge and biases.

\subsubsection{Externally-grounded Verification}
Externally-grounded methods retrieve or check additional evidence
before revising outputs. \textsc{CRITIC}~\cite{gou2024critic},
\textsc{RARR}~\cite{gao2023rarr},
\textsc{Verify-and-Edit}~\cite{zhao2023verifyedit},
\textsc{CRAG}~\cite{yan2024crag},
\textsc{Self-Checker}~\cite{li2024selfchecker}, and
\textsc{Attributable-Trace}~\cite{li2023attribution} ground critique
in retrieved content. \textsc{Self-RAG}~\cite{asai2024selfrag} combines
retrieval and critique tokens in a single decoding loop.

\subsubsection{Process Reward Models}
Process-supervised reward models shift faithfulness from post-hoc
evaluation to a training signal.
\textsc{Lightman PRM}~\cite{lightman2024process}, \textsc{Math-Shepherd}
\cite{wang2023mathshepherd}, \textsc{ReARTeR}~\cite{sun2025rearter},
\textsc{RAG-Gym}~\cite{xiong2025raggym}, and
\textsc{VERITAS}~\cite{xu2025veritas} reward intermediate reasoning
steps or evidence-grounded actions rather than only final correctness.
\textsc{ReST-MCTS}~\cite{zhang2024restmcts} and similar systems combine
search and process supervision.

\subsubsection{Counterfactual and Deletion Tests}
Counterfactual benchmarks such as \textsc{CofCA}~\cite{wu2024cofca}
test whether changing a bridge variable changes the answer. Deletion
tests~\cite{liu2023evaluating,xu2024docnli} remove a candidate
supporting passage and measure whether the answer changes. Both provide
interventional proxies for causal faithfulness, but neither identifies
$\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}$ without additional assumptions.

\subsubsection{Citation and Attribution Models}
Citation-aware generators
\textsc{WebGPT}~\cite{nakano2021webgpt},
\textsc{GopherCite}~\cite{menick2022gophercite}, and
\textsc{ALCE}~\cite{gao2023alce} produce per-claim citations that can
be validated for entailment. Citation precision is necessary but not
sufficient for faithfulness, as a citation can be textually relevant
without being causally used.

Under the estimand view, all of these methods target
$\Delta_{\mathrm{faith}}$. They differ in whether they intervene
pre-hoc, in-decoding, or post-hoc, and in whether the supervision
signal is internal, external, or interventional. We return to causal
faithfulness in detail in Section~\ref{sec:causal_faithfulness}.

\subsection{Joint Estimation in Agentic and Hybrid Systems}
\label{subsec:methods_joint}

Hybrid and agentic systems estimate several quantities simultaneously.
\textsc{Self-RAG}~\cite{asai2024selfrag} decides when to retrieve,
critiques evidence, and generates. \textsc{Search-R1}~\cite{jin2025searchr1}
and \textsc{DeepRAG}~\cite{guan2025deeprag} learn retrieval and
reasoning policies with RL. \textsc{LM--KG hybrids} such as
\textsc{ToG 2.0}~\cite{ma2024tog2}, \textsc{RoG}~\cite{luo2024rog},
\textsc{KAG}~\cite{liang2025kag},
\textsc{HippoRAG}~\cite{gutierrez2024hipporag},
\textsc{LightRAG}~\cite{guo2024lightrag},
\textsc{SubgraphRAG}~\cite{li2024subgraphrag}, and
\textsc{GraphRAG}~\cite{edge2024graphrag} combine structured chain
priors with flexible language reasoning. Multi-agent systems such as
\textsc{MA-RAG}~\cite{nguyen2025marag},
\textsc{ReAgent}~\cite{wang2025reagent}, and
\textsc{Chain-of-Agents}~\cite{zhang2024chainofagents} distribute
decomposition, retrieval, reasoning, and verification across roles.

The advantage of joint estimation is end-to-end adaptability. The cost
is attribution: when answer accuracy improves, it is hard to tell
whether the gain came from observability, selection, exposure, fusion,
or faithfulness. We therefore recommend that future agentic and hybrid
systems report component-level diagnostics aligned with the five
estimands rather than only final answer scores.

\subsection{When Method Families Collide}
\label{subsec:method_family_collision}

Recent systems increasingly combine several families: a method may use
a planner, a dense retriever, a graph index, a long-context reader,
and a verifier. Architectural labels become less informative in such
hybrids. The estimand view avoids this problem by decomposing a hybrid
into the quantities it targets. For example, a graph-augmented agent
may use graph neighbors to improve observability, an \LLM planner to
improve conditional utility, a context packer to improve exposure, and
a verifier to improve faithfulness. The method should then be evaluated
at each link rather than described only as ``GraphRAG'' or
``agentic RAG.''

By Proposition~\ref{prop:bottleneck}, a strong retriever may not
improve grounded accuracy when the reader cannot fuse evidence. A
strong reader may not help when the chain is absent from the pool. A
faithfulness verifier may reject unfaithful answers but not recover
missing evidence. Therefore the most credible empirical papers are
those that show where in the product the gain occurs.

\subsection{Mechanism-Level Analyses as an Internal Estimator}
\label{subsec:methods_mechanism}

The estimands $\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}$, $\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}$,
$\theta_{\mathrm{exp}}$, $\theta_{\mathrm{fus}}$, and
$\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}$ are defined over externally observable
quantities: retrieved pools, selected contexts, exposed positions,
generated answers. A growing line of mechanistic-interpretability work
asks a complementary question: does the chain object exist
\emph{inside} the model, and can we measure it there?
\textsc{Dissecting Multi-Hop}~\cite{geva2023dissecting} traces how
intermediate facts are recalled and combined in transformer layers.
\textsc{Do LLMs Latently Perform Multi-Hop Reasoning?}~\cite{yang2024do}
shows that some models recall bridge entities in middle layers but use
them inconsistently downstream.
\textsc{Hopping}~\cite{biran2024hopping} characterizes step-by-step
recall across layers.
\textsc{Grokked Transformers}~\cite{wang2024grokked} demonstrate that
generalization on multi-hop tasks emerges with extended training and
specific circuit formation.

In the latent-chain view, these analyses provide a third source of
information about $\echain^\star$, in addition to retrieved evidence
and external annotations. They can be read as \emph{internal
estimators} of two quantities: (i) whether $\echain^\star$ or a
proxy of it is representable inside the reader (a necessary condition
for high $\theta_{\mathrm{fus}}$ under any external evidence), and
(ii) whether the answer is produced by combining the intermediate
representations of the chain or by parametric shortcut (an internal
lower bound on $\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}$). Mechanistic analyses are
not yet routinely integrated with retrieval-augmented systems, but
they suggest a future diagnostic layer that combines external
chain-preservation metrics with internal circuit-level evidence use.

{Retrieval-based methods mapped by adaptivity (horizontal) and retrieval-state richness (vertical). Neither axis encodes answer quality or chain observability; the boxes locate mechanism families and list representative systems.}
{fig:retrieval_methods}

Figure~\ref{fig:retrieval_methods} organizes retrieval control by the
degree of adaptivity granted to the evidence-acquisition policy.
Retrieval-based systems commit to enlarging or reshaping $L_N$ as the
primary lever. Single-shot sparse and dense retrievers, iterative and
reasoning-interleaved retrievers, adaptive controllers, hierarchical
and long-context retrievers, and corrective-retrieval variants are
all reviewed as observability estimators in
Section~\ref{subsec:methods_observability}. The architectural
commitment of this family is that better proposal-pool construction is
worth the added cost and drift; its estimand contribution is
$\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}$, with $\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}$ additionally
engaged when the retriever also reranks conditioned on partial state
(where conditional utility is used as the internal mechanism score
driving selection preservation).

\subsection{Graph-based Methods}
\label{subsec:arch_graph}

Graph-based methods commit to structured fusion: paths are
first-class and can be audited hop by hop. Question-specific entity
or document graphs (\textsc{GRAFT-Net},
\textsc{PullNet}, \textsc{DFGN}, \textsc{HGN}), \LM--\GNN couplings
(\textsc{QA-GNN}, \textsc{GreaseLM}, \textsc{UniKGQA},
\textsc{ReaRev}, \textsc{NSM}, \textsc{KG-FiD}), and \LLM-built or
\LLM-traversed graphs (\textsc{ToG}, \textsc{ToG 2.0},
\textsc{RoG}, \textsc{GraphRAG}, \textsc{LightRAG},
\textsc{HippoRAG}, \textsc{SubgraphRAG}, \textsc{KAG}) are reviewed
as observability, fusion, or faithfulness estimators in
Sections~\ref{subsec:methods_observability},
\ref{subsec:methods_fusion}, and~\ref{subsec:methods_faithfulness}.
Additional works that mostly matter for the graph-construction step
rather than for downstream reasoning include
\textsc{SAE}~\cite{tu2020sae},
\textsc{HDE}~\cite{tu2019hde},
\textsc{EmbedKGQA}~\cite{saxena2020embedkgqa},
\textsc{TransferNet}~\cite{shi2021transfernet}, and
\textsc{KGP}~\cite{wang2024kgp}. The family's shared limitation is
that construction quality (missing nodes, noisy entity linking,
incomplete edges) reduces observability before reasoning is invoked;
\LLM-built graphs shift the bottleneck to extraction reliability.

\subsection{Decomposition-, Fusion-, LLM-Reasoning-, and Verification-based Families}
\label{subsec:arch_others}

Four architectural families overlap heavily with Section~\ref{sec:estimand_methods}
and therefore need only a short pointer here.
\emph{Decomposition-based} methods (sub-question, programmatic, and
plan-driven variants) are reviewed as utility and fusion estimators in
Sections~\ref{subsec:methods_utility} and \ref{subsec:methods_fusion};
their bottleneck is cascading error and granularity tuning.
\emph{Fusion-based readers} (retrieve-then-read backbones,
compression-aware fusion, long-context and memory-augmented readers)
are downstream estimators of $\theta_{\mathrm{fus}}$ under
$\theta_{\mathrm{exp}}$; they cannot repair missing evidence and are
reviewed in Section~\ref{subsec:methods_fusion}.
\emph{\LLM-based reasoning} spans linear chain-of-thought, non-linear
topologies (\textsc{ToT}, \textsc{GoT}, \textsc{AoT},
\textsc{SoT}~\cite{ning2024sot},
\textsc{Cumulative Reasoning}~\cite{zhang2024cumulative}), programmatic
reasoning, latent reasoning, tool-use and agentic reasoning
(\textsc{ToolLLM}~\cite{qin2023toolllm},
\textsc{Gorilla}~\cite{patil2023gorilla},
\textsc{Voyager}~\cite{wang2023voyager},
\textsc{Generative Agents}~\cite{park2023agents}), and search-based
reasoning (\textsc{LATS}~\cite{zhou2024lats},
\textsc{Reasoning-via-Planning}~\cite{hao2023reasoning}); each variant
is placed under its primary estimand in Sections~\ref{subsec:methods_observability}
through~\ref{subsec:methods_faithfulness}. \emph{Verification-based}
methods are structurally identical to the causal-faithfulness
estimators of Section~\ref{subsec:methods_faithfulness}
(self-verification, externally grounded verification, process reward
models, counterfactual and deletion tests); the architectural label
adds no independent estimand contribution.

\subsection{Long-context, Multimodal, and Semi-structured Substrates}
\label{subsec:arch_longmultimodal}

Two substrate directions deserve their own architectural note because
their evidence spaces differ from standard text corpora. \emph{Long-context
reasoning} is stressed by \textsc{LongBench}~\cite{bai2024longbench},
\textsc{$\infty$Bench}~\cite{zhang2024infinitybench}, and
\textsc{NovelHopQA}~\cite{novelhopqa2025}, motivated by
lost-in-the-middle effects~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle} and enabled by
\textsc{LongLLaMA}~\cite{tworkowski2023longllama} and
\textsc{Mamba}~\cite{gu2024mamba}; retrieval-side adaptations include
\textsc{LongRAG}~\cite{jiang2024longrag} and
\textsc{RAPTOR}~\cite{sarthi2024raptor}. \emph{Multimodal and tabular
multi-hop} requires alignment across text, tables, and images:
\textsc{MultiModalQA}~\cite{talmor2021multimodalqa},
\textsc{WebQA}~\cite{chang2022webqa},
\textsc{ManyModalQA}~\cite{hannan2020manymodalqa},
\textsc{MMRA}~\cite{lee2024mmra},
\textsc{VisDoM}~\cite{wang2024visdom},
\textsc{DocHop-QA}~\cite{suri2024dochop},
\textsc{WikiMixQA}~\cite{he2024wikimixqa},
\textsc{Img2LLM}~\cite{guo2023img2llm},
\textsc{LLaVA-RAG}~\cite{lin2024llavarag},
\textsc{HybridQA}~\cite{chen2020hybridqa},
\textsc{OTT-QA}~\cite{chen2021opentableqa},
\textsc{TabFact}~\cite{chen2020tabfact},
\textsc{FeTaQA}~\cite{nan2022fetaqa}, and
\textsc{TAT-QA}~\cite{zhu2021tatqa}. These substrates change what
counts as an evidence unit $e$ but leave the five estimands intact;
their main statistical challenge is that both
$\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}$ and $\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}$ become
modality-conditional and typically decrease under cross-modal
composition.

\subsection{Hybrid and Agentic \RAG}
\label{subsec:arch_hybrid}

Hybrid and agentic systems combine several families into a single
policy: \textsc{Self-RAG}, \textsc{Search-R1},
\textsc{R1-Searcher}, \textsc{ReSearch}, \textsc{DeepRAG},
\textsc{MA-RAG}, \textsc{ReAgent},
\textsc{Chain-of-Agents}, and the \KG-integrated variants
(\textsc{KAG}, \textsc{HippoRAG}, \textsc{GraphRAG},
\textsc{LightRAG}, \textsc{SubgraphRAG}) are reviewed as joint
estimators in Section~\ref{subsec:methods_joint}. Their architectural
distinctness lies not in a new estimand but in \emph{attribution
difficulty}: end-to-end gains are entangled across all five events.
By Proposition~\ref{prop:bottleneck}, any headline improvement in
grounded end-to-end success must be traceable to an improvement in
at least one of the five component events. Without per-factor
diagnostics, such a gain cannot be distinguished from a
shortcut-driven improvement in the non-grounded term of
equation~\ref{eq:success_split}; this section therefore treats
hybrid/agentic \RAG as a family that most urgently needs the
estimand-level reporting recommended in
Section~\ref{sec:reporting_guidelines}.

\label{sec:benchmarks}

% =====================================================================

Figure~\ref{fig:benchmark_estimand_alignment} gives the benchmark-level
crosswalk used throughout this section. Benchmarks differ less by domain than by which latent event they make
observable. We organize benchmarks by diagnostic function rather than

\subsection{Coverage and Chain-Observability Diagnostics}
\label{subsec:coverage_diagnostics}

\textsc{HotpotQA}~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa},
\textsc{2WikiMultiHopQA}~\cite{ho2020wikimultihopqa},
\textsc{IIRC}~\cite{ferguson2020iirc},
\textsc{ComplexWebQuestions}~\cite{talmor2018complexwebquestions},
\textsc{QAngaroo}~\cite{welbl2018qangaroo}, and open-domain variants of
multi-hop \QA primarily test whether systems can retrieve enough
evidence to make the latent chain observable. Supporting-fact labels
provide partial observability of $\obs$ and $\sel$, although they do
not always identify the chain actually used by the model. The classic
weakness of \textsc{HotpotQA} is that many examples can be answered
correctly from a single paragraph due to lexical artifacts; the
``distractor setting'' provides better selection diagnostics than the
``full-wiki setting,'' but neither fully isolates membership from
ordering effects.

\subsection{Shortcut-Resistance Diagnostics}
\label{subsec:shortcut_diagnostics}

\textsc{MuSiQue}~\cite{trivedi2022musique} was designed by composing
single-hop questions and filtering shortcuts, making it a stronger
test of true chain observability than datasets where one paragraph may
suffice. \textsc{MoreHopQA}~\cite{schnitzler2024morehopqa} stresses
longer chains, where observability compounds multiplicatively.
\textsc{NovelHopQA}~\cite{novelhopqa2025} uses long-form narrative
sources where chain reasoning interacts with long-context layout.
\textsc{HotpotQA-NoShortcut}~\cite{trivedi2020nohotpot} provides a
filtered subset that requires real multi-hop. These benchmarks are
valuable because they reduce the chance that high answer accuracy
comes from shallow cues rather than successful chain inference.

\subsection{Path and Process Diagnostics}
\label{subsec:path_diagnostics}

\textsc{2WikiMultiHopQA} supplies explicit reasoning paths.
\textsc{EntailmentBank}~\cite{dalvi2021entailmentbank} provides
entailment trees over scientific facts.
\textsc{eQASC}~\cite{jhamtani2020eqasc} provides explanation chains
over commonsense reasoning.
\textsc{ProofWriter}~\cite{tafjord2021proofwriter} provides synthetic
proofs.
\textsc{HoVer}~\cite{jiang2020hover},
\textsc{EX-FEVER}~\cite{ma2024exfever}, and
\textsc{FoolMeTwice}~\cite{eisenschlos2021foolmetwice} annotate
multi-step evidence for verification. These benchmarks help diagnose
$\fus$, because they make some intermediate structure visible rather
than evaluating only final answers.

\subsection{Heterogeneous-Evidence Diagnostics}
\label{subsec:hetero_diagnostics}

\textsc{CWQ}~\cite{talmor2018complexwebquestions},
\textsc{HybridQA}~\cite{chen2020hybridqa},
\textsc{OTT-QA}~\cite{chen2021opentableqa},
\textsc{MultiModalQA}~\cite{talmor2021multimodalqa},
\textsc{WebQA}~\cite{chang2022webqa},
\textsc{ManyModalQA}~\cite{hannan2020manymodalqa},
\textsc{TabFact}~\cite{chen2020tabfact},
\textsc{TAT-QA}~\cite{zhu2021tatqa},
\textsc{FeTaQA}~\cite{nan2022fetaqa},
\textsc{VisDoM}~\cite{wang2024visdom},
\textsc{DocHop-QA}~\cite{suri2024dochop}, and
\textsc{WikiMixQA}~\cite{he2024wikimixqa} stress fusion across
structured and unstructured sources. Their diagnostic value lies less
in raw answer accuracy than in whether a method can maintain the chain
across different evidence types.

\subsection{Counterfactual and Faithfulness Diagnostics}
\label{subsec:faithfulness_diagnostics}

\textsc{CofCA}~\cite{wu2024cofca}, citation-faithfulness benchmarks
\textsc{ALCE}~\cite{gao2023alce}, \textsc{HaluEval}~\cite{li2023halueval},
\textsc{ExpertQA}~\cite{malaviya2024expertqa}, and fact-verification
datasets such as \textsc{FEVER}~\cite{thorne2018fever},
\textsc{HoVer}~\cite{jiang2020hover},
\textsc{EX-FEVER}~\cite{ma2024exfever},
\textsc{SciFact}~\cite{wadden2020scifact}, and
\textsc{AVeriTeC}~\cite{schlichtkrull2024averitec} probe whether the
answer changes when bridge variables or supporting facts are
perturbed. These benchmarks are closest to measuring
$\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}$, because they ask whether evidence is
causally load-bearing rather than merely cited.

\subsection{Long-context Multi-hop Diagnostics}
\label{subsec:longcontext_diagnostics}

\textsc{LongBench}~\cite{bai2024longbench},
\textsc{$\infty$Bench}~\cite{zhang2024infinitybench},
\textsc{HELMET}~\cite{yen2024helmet},
\textsc{NovelHopQA}~\cite{novelhopqa2025},
\textsc{NarrativeQA}~\cite{kovcisky2018narrativeqa},
\textsc{Loong}~\cite{wang2024loong}, and
\textsc{ZeroSCROLLS}~\cite{shaham2023zeroscrolls} stress chain
construction under long context, where exposure failures dominate over
selection failures.

\subsection{RAG-specific and Agentic Diagnostics}
\label{subsec:rag_specific_diagnostics}

\textsc{MultiHop-RAG}~\cite{tang2024multihoprag},
\textsc{FRAMES}~\cite{krishna2024frames},
\textsc{CRAG}~\cite{yang2024crag},
\textsc{FanOutQA}~\cite{zhu2024fanoutqa}, and
\textsc{RAGTruth}~\cite{niu2024ragtruth} evaluate end-to-end \RAG
behavior under multi-step queries.
\textsc{ToolBench}~\cite{qin2023toolbench} and
\textsc{AgentBench}~\cite{liu2024agentbench} test agentic search
behavior.
\textsc{WebArena}~\cite{zhou2024webarena} introduces realistic

\subsection{Metric Alignment}
\label{subsec:metric_alignment}

A metric should be matched to the estimand it claims to measure.
Answer EM and F1 are deployment-level metrics, not mechanism-level
metrics. Supporting-fact F1 approximates evidence membership but does
not establish causal use. Retrieval recall at Top-$N$ estimates pool
observability but says little about the final reader context. Citation
precision estimates attribution correctness but can be high even when
the answer was produced from parametric memory. Counterfactual
sensitivity is closer to causal faithfulness but depends on the
quality of the intervention.

\subsection{Dataset Families Reinterpreted}
\label{subsec:dataset_families_reinterpreted}

Traditional benchmark descriptions emphasize source domain and release
year. The estimand view emphasizes which latent event a benchmark
makes measurable. \textsc{HotpotQA} is valuable because sentence-level
support labels enable pool and context diagnostics, but its shortcut
artifacts mean that answer accuracy can overstate chain use.
\textsc{2WikiMultiHopQA} adds explicit path structure, making it useful
for path-level observability and fusion analysis. \textsc{MuSiQue} is
valuable because it reduces single-hop shortcuts and stresses genuine
bridge observability. \textsc{IIRC} makes missing context explicit,
placing retrieval actions at the center. \textsc{FEVER},
\textsc{HoVer}, and \textsc{EX-FEVER} foreground support validity
rather than answer-string matching. \textsc{HybridQA} and
\textsc{MultiModalQA} stress fusion because a correct chain may cross
tables, linked passages, and other semi-structured sources.

This reinterpretation changes how benchmark coverage should be reported.
A paper that evaluates only on shortcut-prone datasets primarily shows
that the system can exploit available signals; it does not establish
robust latent-chain inference. A paper that evaluates only on
verification datasets may test faithfulness without testing open-domain
chain observability. A strong empirical section should therefore
include at least one benchmark for observability, one for shortcut
resistance, and one for faithfulness or counterfactual sensitivity.

row. In \emph{enterprise RAG}, the traceability requirement is
regulatory rather than statistical: even when

adequate, deployments still require per-answer citation surfaces that
survive compliance review, which is why graph-indexed pipelines
dominate the space~\cite{edge2024graphrag,guo2024lightrag}. In
\emph{scientific literature assistance}, the fusion challenge is
non-standard because evidence units differ not only in surface form
(text, table, figure) but in epistemic status (claim, method,
result), and a chain that composes a claim from one paper with a
result from another can be internally coherent yet scientifically
invalid; citation- and claim-level counterfactual checks are the
current best proxy for this hazard. Both cases are consistent with
the estimand vocabulary --- they simply move the binding constraint
outside the numerical decomposition.

\section{Faithfulness as Causal Evidence Use}
\label{sec:causal_faithfulness}


% =====================================================================

We argued in Section~\ref{subsec:causal_estimand} that faithfulness is
a causal estimand. A high-quality multi-hop \RAG system should produce
answers that are causally supported by the retrieved chain rather than
answers that merely co-occur with plausible-looking citations. This
section develops the causal view in detail, connects it to existing
faithfulness diagnostics, and explains why several common metrics are
necessary but not sufficient. We also use the running example
(Section~\ref{subsec:running_example}) to illustrate each failure mode.

\subsection{A Causal Graph for Multi-Hop \RAG}
\label{subsec:causal_graph}

Let $W$ denote the world state, $\kstore$ the knowledge store derived
from $W$, $\echain^\star$ the latent support chain, $L_N$ the retrieved
pool, $E_K$ the selected context, $\pi(E_K)$ the ordering, and
$\hat{a}$ the answer. A simple causal graph for a faithful system is
\begin{equation}
W\rightarrow \kstore\rightarrow \echain^\star\rightarrow L_N
\rightarrow E_K\rightarrow \pi(E_K)\rightarrow \hat a,
\end{equation}
with the parametric memory of the reader $\Theta$ acting as an
exogenous influence on $\hat a$:
\begin{equation}
\Theta\rightarrow \hat a.
\end{equation}
A causally faithful answer is one whose distribution responds to
interventions on $\echain^\star$ through $\pi(E_K)$, i.e.
\begin{equation}
P(\hat a\mid \doop(\pi(E_K)=\pi'))
\neq P(\hat a\mid \pi(E_K))
\quad\text{whenever}\quad
P(a^\star\mid \pi(E_K))\neq P(a^\star\mid \pi').
\end{equation}
An unfaithful answer can be partitioned into two cases: \emph{parametric
leakage}, in which $\hat a$ is generated through $\Theta$ alone and is
invariant under context interventions; and \emph{shortcut use}, in
which $\hat a$ depends on a non-evidence feature of $\pi(E_K)$ such as
template wording, length, or surface lexical overlap.

\subsection{Deletion and Counterfactual Sensitivity}
\label{subsec:deletion_counterfactual}

Two related interventions probe causal use.

\paragraph{Deletion sensitivity.} For each selected evidence unit
$e\in E_K$, define
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{del}}(e)
= P(\hat a=a^\star\mid \pi(E_K))-P(\hat a=a^\star\mid \pi(E_K\setminus\{e\})).
\end{equation}
A faithful answer should exhibit a positive deletion sensitivity on
support units of the latent chain. \textsc{Sufficient evidence}
metrics~\cite{liu2023evaluating} and the \textsc{Attributable-Trace}
framework~\cite{li2023attribution} implement variants of this idea.

\paragraph{Counterfactual bridge sensitivity.} Let $e_b^\star$ be a
bridge unit whose factual content can be altered to obtain a
counterfactual version $e_b'$ encoding a different but consistent
alternative (e.g., replacing the bridge identity ``Shirley Temple''
with another cast member and updating the downstream biography evidence
consistently).
Counterfactual benchmarks~\cite{wu2024cofca} test whether the answer
shifts accordingly. A reader that answers correctly regardless of
$e_b'$ is exploiting parametric memory; a reader whose answer shifts
to remain consistent with the counterfactual bridge is using the
chain causally.

\subsection{Correctness, Citation, and Faithfulness Are Distinct}
\label{subsec:correctness_citation_faithfulness}

A common methodological mistake is to treat citation correctness and
answer correctness as faithfulness. They are necessary signals but not
sufficient.

\begin{remark}[Three independent failure modes]
\label{rem:three_failure_modes}
For each example, the joint outcome
$(\hat a\stackrel{?}{=}a^\star,\, \text{citations valid?},\,
\hat a\stackrel{?}{\leftarrow}\echain^\star)$ takes one of eight
truth-value combinations. Six of the eight are pathological. Of
particular interest are: correct answer with valid citation but no
causal use (parametric leakage); correct answer without valid citation
(citation suppression); valid citation with wrong answer (citation
without composition). A single number such as answer F1 or citation
precision cannot distinguish these cases.
\end{remark}

A practical recommendation follows: faithfulness audits should report
all three signals jointly, or at minimum stratify answer correctness
by citation validity and by deletion sensitivity. Papers that report
only one of the three numbers should be read with this caveat in mind.

\subsection{Shortcut Exploitation and Parametric Leakage}
\label{subsec:shortcut_parametric}

Shortcut and leakage failures are not equally easy to detect.
Shortcut exploitation usually leaves a measurable trace: removing a
shortcut feature (such as the surface form of an answer entity in the
question) changes accuracy. \textsc{MuSiQue}~\cite{trivedi2022musique}
and shortcut-filtered subsets of \textsc{HotpotQA}~\cite{trivedi2020nohotpot}
were constructed precisely to reduce this bias.

Parametric leakage is harder to detect because the leaked answer is
plausibly produced by the chain. Closed-book and oracle-evidence
ablations are useful but incomplete: closed-book accuracy estimates
parametric availability, but a model can still leak even when oracle
evidence is provided. Counterfactual benchmarks are the closest
approximation, because they make leakage observable through the
divergence between counterfactual chain and observed answer.

\subsection{From Verification to Training Signal}
\label{subsec:verification_training}

Faithfulness has historically been treated as a post-hoc evaluation.
Recent work moves it upstream into training. \textsc{Math-Shepherd}
\cite{wang2023mathshepherd} and the process reward model of
\textsc{Lightman et al.}~\cite{lightman2024process} reward
intermediate reasoning steps rather than only final answers.
\textsc{ReARTeR}~\cite{sun2025rearter} trains a trustworthy process
reward model tailored to retrieval-augmented reasoning.
\textsc{VERITAS}~\cite{xu2025veritas} uses faithfulness-aware reward
signals. \textsc{RAG-Gym}~\cite{xiong2025raggym} adds process
supervision to search agents. \textsc{PAR-RAG}~\cite{zhang2025parrag}
inserts a review stage that catches drift before it propagates.

From the latent-chain view, all of these methods convert faithfulness
from an evaluation property into an objective. They differ in whether
the reward is supplied by humans, by a verifier, or by self-critique;
in whether the reward is per-step or per-trajectory; and in whether the
training data includes interventional examples. Combining process
supervision with counterfactual evaluation appears to be one of the
most promising directions for measurable faithfulness gains.

\section{Statistical Tradeoffs and Open Problems}
\label{sec:tradeoffs}
% =====================================================================

The latent-chain view reorganizes open problems as tradeoffs among
estimands. Each tradeoff is a constraint: improving one estimand often
worsens another. A successful method does not eliminate the tradeoff;
it moves the system to a better point on the frontier.

\subsection{Eight Open Problems}
\label{subsec:open_problems}

\paragraph{OP1. Joint optimization of observability and noise.}
Iterative and agentic retrieval increase $\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}$ but
inflate $\rho_N$. No widely accepted method jointly minimizes
expected risk under a budget. Open directions include retrieval
policies trained against joint chain-coverage and noise-ratio
objectives, and verifier-in-the-loop pruning that operates on the
pool rather than on the reader's output.

\paragraph{OP2. Conditional utility estimation under chain dependence.}
Most rerankers estimate marginal relevance. Conditional utility
estimation under partial chains is still rare and is hard to
supervise. Open directions include core-conditioned scoring,
bridge-classification objectives, and synthetic counterfactual chains
for utility regression training.

\paragraph{OP3. Reader-aware exposure under long context.}
Long-context models have not eliminated positional
bias~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle,yen2024lcontext}. Open directions include
fixed-membership ordering policies, decoder-side fusion (\FiD
variants), context compression, and explicit attention scaffolds.

\paragraph{OP4. Auditable latent reasoning.}
Latent reasoning methods~\cite{goyal2024quietstar,hao2024coconut}
trade chain visibility for inference efficiency. Faithfulness audits
based on emitted tokens cease to apply. Open directions include
mechanistic probes for internal bridge representations and hybrid
latent--token reasoning where the chain is occasionally externalized.

\paragraph{OP5. Causal evaluation for retrieval-augmented systems.}
Counterfactual evaluation is expensive. Most benchmarks still use
factual data only. Open directions include synthetic counterfactual
chain generation, deletion-based attribution at the chain level
rather than at the token level, and joint citation--causal-use
metrics.

\paragraph{OP6. Knowledge update without catastrophic faithfulness loss.}
Retrieval is a natural mechanism for keeping knowledge current, but
edits in $\kstore$ can conflict with parametric memory. Open
directions include retrieval policies that detect knowledge-update
events, and reader objectives that prefer retrieved evidence over
parametric memory under conflict~\cite{xie2024adaptive,
mallen2023whentoretrieve}.

\paragraph{OP7. Cost-aware multi-hop \RAG.}
Agentic and iterative methods increase latency and token cost. A
deployable system must trade these against expected chain
observability. Open directions include cost-sensitive policy learning,
calibrated retrieval termination, and small-model verifiers paired
with strong readers.

\paragraph{OP8. Safety and robustness under retrieval-side adversaries.}
Retrieval-side prompt injection and corpus poisoning can compromise
otherwise faithful systems~\cite{zou2024poisonedrag,
xue2024badrag,zhong2023poisoning}. Open directions include trust-aware
retrieval, multi-source agreement, and adversarial training for
retrievers.

\section{Reporting Guidelines for Multi-Hop RAG}
\label{sec:reporting_guidelines}
% =====================================================================

The latent-chain view directly implies a small set of reporting
guidelines. We collect them here as a checklist for papers and
reviewers.

\subsection{Separate Pool, Context, and Reader Claims}
\label{subsec:separate_claims}

A paper introducing a new multi-hop \RAG system should explicitly
distinguish three claims: a claim about the retrieved pool $L_N$, a
claim about the budgeted context $E_K$, and a claim about the reader.
Reporting only end-to-end accuracy can hide compensating effects.
Concretely, we recommend at least one number from each of the
following: pool-level support or path recall; context-level chain
preservation under the budget actually used; and reader correctness
under both natural and oracle or near-oracle contexts.

\subsection{Report Diagnostics on Conditional Subsets}
\label{subsec:conditional_subsets}

Diagnostics should respect the nested-event structure of
equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp}. Faithfulness numbers should be
reported on the chain-present subset; exposure ablations should hold
membership fixed; fusion ablations should hold both membership and
ordering fixed. Numbers that mix multiple conditions hide the channel
of improvement and reduce reproducibility.

\subsection{Always Report Budget and Cost}
\label{subsec:budget_cost}

Agentic and iterative methods can improve accuracy by spending more
tokens, more retrieval calls, or more verifier calls. Without
budget-normalized numbers, comparison is unfair. A minimal cost report
includes: total tokens per question (prompt and generation), retrieval
calls per question, verifier calls per question, and end-to-end
latency. Where possible, accuracy-cost curves should replace single
operating points.

\subsection{Distinguish Gold-Used and Oracle Support}
\label{subsec:gold_used}

Gold supporting facts in benchmarks are not necessarily the facts a
system used. Two systems with the same answer can use different
chains; one chain may be valid and the other may exploit a shortcut.
Reporting whether the system's actual evidence contains the gold
supports, together with how often it differs, helps distinguish
faithful and shortcut behavior.

\label{subsec:min_reporting_table}

\subsection{Threats to the Latent-Chain View}
\label{subsec:threats_view}

For completeness, we list four limitations of our framing. First,
$\echain^\star$ is set-valued, not unique; some questions admit many
valid chains, and partial supervision systematically undercounts them.
Second, latent-reasoning methods (Section~\ref{subsec:latent_mechanism})
push chain inference into model internals, where external diagnostics
do not apply without additional probes. Third, agentic systems may
exhibit emergent behavior that is not well captured by any fixed
factorization. Fourth, our success-factor decomposition
(equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp}) is exact only as a chain-rule
expansion over a particular nested event sequence; under parametric
leakage and shortcut events, the relationship to end-to-end accuracy
is one of upper bound rather than equality. We have flagged this in
the relevant theorem and remarks, and recommend that empirical work
treat the decomposition as a diagnostic tool rather than as a strict
prediction.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================

This survey recasts multi-hop \RAG as inference over a latent support
chain observed through a noisy retrieval pool, budgeted context,
evidence ordering, and generated answer. Five conditional coordinates---
observability, selection preservation, exposure, fusion, and causal
faithfulness---complement architectural taxonomies with a diagnostic
account of failure. Equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp} and
Proposition~\ref{prop:bottleneck} shows why grounded success is jointly
limited and why empirical claims must identify the component event
that changed. Conditional evidence utility remains a mechanism-level
score for selection rather than an extra stage probability.

Evaluation should therefore be conditional: faithfulness on chain-absent
examples conflates retrieval and attribution, while fusion on
chain-missing examples conflates selection and reasoning. Reporting
should expose all five factors even when a method targets only one.
Promising systems will improve several factors without concealing a
loss in another, for example through process-supervised retrieval,
bridge-aware selection, or counterfactual training.

Agentic search reformulates evidence acquisition as policy learning;
long-context readers change evidence granularity; latent-reasoning
methods move fusion into hidden states. None removes the unobserved
support chain, and each introduces distinct attribution or auditability
risks. Naming that persistent object explicitly provides a stable basis
for comparing the next generation of multi-hop \RAG systems.

\section{Mathematical Notes}\label{app:math}

\subsection*{Granularity of evidence units}

In Definition~\ref{def:generative_chain}, evidence units \(e_i\) can be passages,
sentences, table rows, image regions, code blocks, or
knowledge-graph triples. The framework is applicable across evidence
granularities, but the numerical estimands are granularity-dependent:
refining a passage into sentences typically shortens the chain length
required for correctness while altering the marginal retrieval
probability, the budgeted selection probability, and the exposure
distribution of each unit. Comparisons across granularities are
therefore only meaningful when the evaluation unit is held fixed.
Mixed-granularity chains (e.g., a sentence linked to a table row via a
shared entity) are permitted; the bridge variable then ranges over a
heterogeneous space that includes entities, dates, numeric matches,
and image-text grounding links.

\subsection*{Bridge priors and observability}

Chain observability \(\obs(q)\) marginalizes over chain orderings.
Under the often-reasonable assumption that the underlying chain is a
directed acyclic graph rather than a strict sequence, observability is
properly redefined as
\[
\obs(q) = \Pr\bigl(\{e_1,\dots,e_L\} \subseteq \mathcal{P} \mid q\bigr),
\]
and selection preservation, exposure, fusion, and faithfulness factor
analogously. We keep the sequential form in the main text because
all benchmarks we surveyed annotate ordered or unordered chains rather
than richer DAG structures.

\subsection*{Decomposition under chain ambiguity}

When multiple distinct chains support the same answer, the events in
equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp} should be read existentially: each
stage requirement is defined as ``there exists a valid minimal chain
$C\in\chainset^\star(q)$ for which the stage condition holds'' (chain
observable in $L_N$, chain preserved in $E_K$, chain exposed within
depth $r$, chain correctly composed, chain causally used). This is
the form used by oracle-style evaluators that count any complete
supporting chain as correct. Under this existential reading, the
chain-rule factorization of equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp} remains
an \emph{exact} decomposition of the corresponding grounded-success
event, and the bottleneck upper bound in
Proposition~\ref{prop:bottleneck} continues to apply because grounded
success is still bounded above by the smallest of its five conditional
stage factors.

\subsection*{Identifiability under partial supervision}

Proposition~\ref{prop:identifiability} states that \(\fus\) is identifiable
conditional on \((\obs,\sel,\expo)=(1,1,1)\). Under partial supervision,
where only a subset of bridge variables is annotated, identifiability
weakens but does not collapse: \(\fus\) is identifiable up to permutation
on annotated bridges and remains a lower bound on true fusion
reliability on unannotated bridges, by an argument analogous to that of
Yang et al. (2024) for hop-resolved attention attribution.

\subsection*{Log-scale decomposition and additive risk}

Taking logs of equation~\ref{eq:success_decomp} yields the exact
identity
\[
\log P(Y_g\mid q)
\;=\;
\log\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}
+\log\theta_{\mathrm{sel}}
+\log\theta_{\mathrm{exp}}
+\log\theta_{\mathrm{fus}}
+\log\theta_{\mathrm{faith}}.
\]
This form motivates an \emph{additive shortfall} bookkeeping view:
each conditional factor contributes a non-negative shortfall
\(-\log\theta_i\) to the total log deficit, and the largest shortfall
identifies which factor is currently rate-limiting for \(q\). The
factors are conditional probabilities along a nested chain of events,
not marginal probabilities of independent modules; the additive
identity should therefore be read as a diagnostic ranking device
rather than as a claim that the underlying stages are statistically
independent. Practitioners can estimate each shortfall on the
diagnostic subset that reaches the preceding event and rank remediation
targets accordingly.

\section{Writing and Reporting Checklist}\label{app:checklist}

We summarize the reporting guidelines of Section~\ref{sec:reporting_guidelines} as a
checklist that authors of new multi-hop \RAG methods can apply before
submission.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Estimand identification.} State which of the five estimands
the proposed method primarily targets, and which it might inadvertently
affect.
\item \textbf{Conditional metrics.} Report retrieval coverage on
chain-present examples, reader accuracy on chain-present examples,
faithfulness on chain-present-and-attended examples, and corresponding
chain-absent baselines.
\item \textbf{Budget.} Report retrieved-token budget, prompt-token
budget, total \LLM calls, and wall-clock latency per query.
\item \textbf{Used vs.\ gold support.} Report which gold passages were
present in the context and which were attended to, distinguished from
the full pool.
\item \textbf{Process annotations.} If the benchmark provides path or
process labels, report path-level metrics in addition to end-to-end
metrics.
\item \textbf{Counterfactual probes.} If feasible, report sensitivity to
deletion or substitution of bridge evidence.
\item \textbf{Failure analysis.} Provide a small table breaking down
errors by latent-chain failure mode (\(\obs\)-loss, \(\sel\)-loss,
\(\expo\)-loss, \(\fus\)-loss, \(\faith\)-loss).
\item \textbf{Threats.} State the threats to validity of the
latent-chain framing for the chosen benchmarks (Section~\ref{sec:reporting_guidelines}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Claim Templates}\label{app:claims}

For each estimand we provide a template that authors can use to phrase
empirical claims unambiguously. The templates are intentionally
verbose; their purpose is to expose the conditioning assumptions that
remain implicit in the common ``\emph{method X improves multi-hop
accuracy by $y$ points}'' phrasing.

\begin{description}
\item[Observability claim.]
``On queries whose annotated chain is contained in pool \(\mathcal{P}\)
with probability at least \(\alpha\) (under retrieval depth \(k\)), our
method increases this probability from \(\alpha\) to \(\alpha'\); we do
not claim improvement on queries whose chain is unobservable at depth
\(k\).''
\item[Selection-utility claim.]
``Conditional on chain observability (\(\obs=1\)), our method increases
the probability that all chain elements appear in the reader's context
window from \(\beta\) to \(\beta'\); the increment is reported under
fixed token budget \(B\) and reported reranker class.''
\item[Exposure claim.]
``Conditional on selection (\(\sel=1\)), our method increases the
probability that all chain elements are attended to during reading from
\(\gamma\) to \(\gamma'\), measured by attention-weight masking
experiments at threshold \(\tau\).''
\item[Fusion claim.]
``Conditional on observability, selection, and exposure
(\(\obs=\sel=\expo=1\)), our method increases the probability of
producing the correct answer from \(\delta\) to \(\delta'\). The
remaining failures are attributed to fusion under the assumptions of
Section~\ref{sec:problem_setup}.''
\item[Faithfulness claim.]
``Conditional on a correct answer being produced, our method increases
the probability that the answer is causally supported by the cited
evidence chain from \(\epsilon\) to \(\epsilon'\); causal support is
measured by counterfactual deletion sensitivity at perturbation
budget \(\rho\).''
\end{description}

Each template specifies (i) the conditioning event, (ii) the population
of queries, (iii) the budget and protocol, and (iv) the auxiliary
assumptions. We argue that adoption of this style would substantially
reduce comparability problems across the multi-hop \RAG literature.
